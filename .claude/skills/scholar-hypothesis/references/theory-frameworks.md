# Theoretical Frameworks for Social Science Research

## Core Sociological Theories

### Stratification and Inequality

**Status Attainment Model** (Blau and Duncan 1967; Sewell, Haller, Portes 1969)
- Core claim: Occupational attainment is a function of background characteristics and educational attainment
- Mechanisms: Credential signaling, skill acquisition, social influence from significant others
- Best for: Intergenerational mobility, education-earnings linkage
- Key papers: Blau & Duncan (1967 AJS); Sewell et al. (1969 AJS)

**Cultural Capital** (Bourdieu 1984, 1986)
- Core claim: Cultural knowledge, skills, and dispositions are unequally distributed and converted into educational and economic advantage
- Three forms: Embodied, objectified, institutionalized
- Mechanisms: Institutional matching (elite gatekeepers favor elite cultural tastes), transmission through family
- Best for: Education inequality, occupational sorting, class reproduction
- Key papers: Bourdieu (1984 Distinction); DiMaggio (1982 ASR)

**Social Closure** (Weber; Parkin 1979; Murphy 1988)
- Core claim: Dominant groups monopolize valued resources by excluding outsiders through credentials, licenses, or group membership
- Mechanisms: Credential inflation, occupational licensing, informal exclusion
- Best for: Professions, racial/ethnic economic inequality, credentialism

**Cumulative Advantage / Matthew Effect** (DiPrete and Eirich 2006; Merton 1968)
- Core claim: Early advantages compound over time; inequality grows through self-reinforcing processes
- Best for: Life course inequality, educational careers, career mobility

---

### Social Networks and Capital

**Strength of Weak Ties** (Granovetter 1973)
- Core claim: Weak ties (acquaintances) bridge otherwise disconnected network clusters and provide access to non-redundant information
- Mechanisms: Information brokerage, bridging social capital
- Best for: Job search, information diffusion, social integration
- Key paper: Granovetter (1973 AJS)

**Structural Holes** (Burt 1992)
- Core claim: Individuals who bridge disconnected groups gain informational and positional advantages
- Mechanisms: Information brokerage, control over resource flows
- Best for: Organizational advantage, career success, innovation
- Key paper: Burt (1992 Structural Holes)

**Social Capital** (Lin 2001; Coleman 1988; Putnam 2000)
- Lin: Resources embedded in social networks that can be mobilized through ties
- Coleman: Trust, norms, network closure that facilitate collective action
- Putnam: Bonding vs. bridging social capital at community level
- Best for: Mobility, community resilience, civic participation

**Network Diffusion** (Centola and Macy 2007; Watts 1999)
- Core claim: Information and behaviors spread through network structures; complex behaviors require wide bridges, not just bridges
- Best for: Health behavior diffusion, social movements, technology adoption

---

### Cultural and Meaning-Making Theories

**Symbolic Boundaries** (Lamont 1992; Lamont and Molnár 2002)
- Core claim: Groups construct moral and cultural boundaries to define who belongs and who doesn't
- Types: Moral, socioeconomic, cultural boundaries
- Best for: Intergroup relations, racial attitudes, national identity
- Key paper: Lamont (1992 Money, Morals, and Manners)

**Frames and Schemas** (Goffman 1974; DiMaggio 1997; Vaisey 2009)
- Goffman: Frames organize experience and interpretation
- DiMaggio: Schemas are cognitive structures activated by contexts
- Vaisey: Dual-process; moral schemas motivate action, discursive frames justify post-hoc
- Best for: Survey response processes, media framing, moral reasoning

**Institutional Logics** (Friedland and Alford 1991; Thornton et al. 2012)
- Core claim: Different institutional orders (market, religion, family, state) provide distinct values, goals, and practices
- Best for: Organizational behavior, work-family conflict, professional norms

---

### Immigration and Assimilation

**Straight-Line Assimilation** (Gordon 1964)
- Core claim: Immigrants gradually assimilate into mainstream society across generations
- Stages: Cultural → structural → marital → identificational assimilation
- Best for: Historical immigration, European immigrants to US

**Segmented Assimilation** (Portes and Zhou 1993; Portes and Rumbaut 2001)
- Core claim: Second-generation immigrants assimilate into different segments of society depending on co-ethnic community, family context, and structural reception
- Three paths: Upward assimilation, downward assimilation, selective acculturation
- Best for: Contemporary immigration, racial/ethnic minorities, second generation

**New Assimilation Theory** (Alba and Nee 2003)
- Core claim: Boundaries between immigrant groups and mainstream shift over time through institutional mechanisms
- Mechanisms: Boundary blurring, boundary shifting, boundary crossing
- Best for: Contemporary assimilation, racial/ethnic identity change

**Transnationalism** (Glick Schiller et al. 1992; Portes et al. 1999)
- Core claim: Immigrants maintain sustained connections to origin country while settling in destination
- Best for: Remittances, identity, political mobilization, return migration

---

### Linguistic and Language Theories

**Linguistic Capital** (Bourdieu 1991)
- Core claim: Languages and language varieties have differential market value; dominant language = linguistic capital
- Mechanisms: Legitimate language, symbolic power, field-specific exchange
- Best for: Language and inequality, accent discrimination, educational language policies

**Language Ideologies** (Woolard 1998; Irvine and Gal 2000)
- Core claim: Beliefs about language structure social hierarchies and justify language policies
- Best for: Language policy, bilingualism attitudes, language discrimination

**Accommodation Theory** (Giles 1973; Giles and Powesland 1975)
- Core claim: Speakers adjust their speech style (convergence/divergence) to signal social identity and manage impressions
- Best for: Accent modification, code-switching, intergroup contact

**Language Shift and Maintenance** (Fishman 1991; Grenoble and Whaley 2006)
- Core claim: Language shift occurs when speakers shift to a dominant language across generations; maintenance requires active community support
- Best for: Heritage languages, minority language vitality, bilingual education

---

### Life Course Theory

**Life Course Perspective** (Elder 1994; Elder and Giele 2009)
- Five principles: Historical time and place, timing, linked lives, human agency, life span development
- Best for: Longitudinal analysis, cohort effects, biographical approaches

**Cumulative Disadvantage** (DiPrete and Eirich 2006)
- Disadvantages compound; each setback increases probability of future setbacks
- Best for: Health disparities, educational careers, incarceration effects

---

### Computational and Quantitative Theory

**Structural Equation Modeling Framework**: Mediation and moderation as causal structures
**Social influence models**: Peer effects, network endogeneity
**Bayesian updating**: How individuals revise beliefs in response to information
**Matching and sorting models**: Labor market matching, educational sorting

## Analytical Sociology — Mechanism Specification

### Coleman's Macro-Micro-Macro Boat (Coleman 1990)

**Core claim**: Social phenomena cannot be explained at the macro level alone; explanation requires tracing the path from macro conditions → individual situations → individual actions → macro outcomes.

**The "boat" structure**:
```
Macro cause ──────────────────────────────► Macro effect
     │                                            ▲
     │ (situational mechanism)    (aggregation)   │
     ▼                                            │
Individual situation ──────────────────► Individual action
                     (action mechanism)
```

**Three bridging mechanisms** (Hedström & Swedberg 1998):
1. **Situational**: How macro conditions shape individual situations (e.g., recession → job insecurity)
2. **Action**: How individuals respond given their situation (desire, belief, opportunity)
3. **Aggregation**: How many individuals' actions sum to macro outcomes (e.g., marriage delay → fertility decline)

**Best for**: Any paper that needs to connect structural causes to individual behavior to macro outcomes; explains the mechanism, not just the association.

**Key papers**: Coleman (1990 *Foundations of Social Theory*); Hedström & Swedberg (1998 *Social Mechanisms*); Hedström & Ylikoski (2010 *Annual Review*)

---

### Hedström's DBO Model (Hedström 2005)

**Core claim**: Social action is driven by **Desires** (what actors want), **Beliefs** (what actors believe about the situation and actions), and **Opportunities** (what actions are feasible). These three components jointly determine action.

| Component | Questions to ask | Example (credential inflation) |
|-----------|-----------------|-------------------------------|
| **Desires (D)** | What does the actor want? What are their goals? | Secure employment, status, social belonging |
| **Beliefs (B)** | What does the actor believe about the situation? About what actions produce what outcomes? | "More education → better jobs"; "employers value credentials" |
| **Opportunities (O)** | What options are available? What constraints operate? | Availability of higher education; tuition costs |
| **Action (A)** | Given D + B + O, what does the actor do? | Obtain advanced degree even when not strictly required |
| **Aggregation** | How do many such actions produce the macro outcome? | → Credential inflation; rising education requirements |

**Best for**: Labor markets, education decisions, migration, marriage timing — any decision-based mechanism.

---

### Elster's Mechanism Types (Elster 1989, 1999)

Use to classify the mechanism in your theory for precision:

| Type | Definition | Example |
|------|-----------|---------|
| **Cognitive** | How actors perceive, categorize, or interpret information | Schema activation, stereotyping, framing, sensemaking |
| **Motivational** | How incentives, expectations, or emotions drive behavior | Expected utility, fear of failure, status anxiety |
| **Interactional** | How actors respond to and shape each other | Social comparison, peer effects, reference groups, contagion |
| **Institutional** | How formal and informal rules, roles, and enforcement structure action | Credential requirements, organizational policy, legal rules |
| **Material** | How resource access, constraints, or accumulation operate | Wealth inheritance, geographic proximity, time scarcity |

**Hypothesis language**: "We classify this as a [type] mechanism: [X] affects actors' [beliefs/incentives/resources/rules], which in turn changes [action A], producing [outcome Y]."

## Racial Formation and Racial Inequality Theories

### Racial Formation Theory (Omi and Winant 1994)

**Core claim**: Race is not a fixed biological category but a socially and historically constructed phenomenon — a "floating signifier" whose meaning is contested through political and cultural struggles. "Racial projects" articulate representations of race with the distribution of resources.

**Key concepts**:
- **Racial formation**: The sociohistorical process by which racial categories are created, transformed, and destroyed
- **Racial project**: Any representation of race that links that representation to a distribution of resources (e.g., Jim Crow laws; affirmative action; colorblindness ideology)
- **Racial state**: State institutions actively produce racial categories and enforce racial order
- **Colorblind racism** (Bonilla-Silva 2003): Racial inequality maintained without overt racism through "frames" like naturalization, cultural racism, minimization of racism, abstract liberalism

**Best for**: Race and labor markets, education, incarceration, health; media representations; immigration policy; institutional discrimination

**Hypothesis language**:
- "We hypothesize that [institutional practice] operates as a racial project, systematically directing resources away from [group] through [mechanism], producing [disparity outcome]."
- "Colorblind ideology leads [actors] to attribute [disparity] to [cultural/individual factors] rather than structural causes, predicting [outcome] at the institutional level."

**Key papers**: Omi & Winant (1994 *Racial Formation in the United States*); Bonilla-Silva (2003 *Racism Without Racists*)

---

### Categorical Inequality (Tilly 1998)

**Core claim**: Durable inequality arises not from individual attributes but from the categorical distinctions that organizations use — paired categories (male/female, citizen/non-citizen, credentialed/non-credentialed) systematically channel resources toward one category.

**Mechanisms**:
- **Exploitation**: One category extracts value from another's efforts
- **Opportunity hoarding**: One category monopolizes access to a valued resource
- **Emulation**: Organizations copy categorical distinctions from successful models
- **Adaptation**: Daily routines adapt to and reinforce categorical distinctions

**Best for**: Persistent labor market inequality; organizational discrimination; institutional racism; gender wage gap

**Key paper**: Tilly (1998 *Durable Inequality*)

---

### Status Characteristics and Expectation States Theory (Berger, Webster, Ridgeway)

**Core claim**: In group interaction, actors use **diffuse status characteristics** (race, gender, education, age) as performance expectations. Higher-status actors receive more speaking turns, more positive evaluations, and greater influence — even when status characteristics are irrelevant to the task.

**Key concepts**:
- **Diffuse status characteristics**: Generalized status signals that actors apply across contexts
- **Specific status characteristics**: Domain-specific ability evaluations
- **Expectation states**: Actors' beliefs about relative task competence, which guide participation and influence
- **Status generalization**: The process by which status characteristics from one domain spread to new contexts

**Mechanisms**:
1. Status characteristics are activated in group settings
2. Actors form performance expectations based on status signals
3. Higher performance expectations → more participation, more positive evaluations, more influence

**Best for**: Group decision-making, evaluation bias, organizational hierarchies, gender and race in workplaces, credentialing effects

**Hypothesis language**:
- "We hypothesize that [status characteristic Z] operates as a diffuse status cue, leading evaluators to assign lower [performance expectations / credibility / evaluations] to [lower-status group] members, independent of [task-relevant attributes]."
- "The interaction of [race] and [gender] produces the lowest performance expectations for [intersection group] through additive status generalization, predicting the lowest [outcome] for that group."

**Key papers**: Berger et al. (1977); Ridgeway (1991 ASR); Webster & Foschi (1988)

## Social Movements and Collective Action

### Political Process / Political Opportunity Structure (McAdam 1982; Tarrow 1994)

**Core claim**: Social movements emerge and succeed when **political opportunities** expand (weakened state, elite divisions, realignments, allies) AND when **mobilizing structures** (organizations, networks) and **collective action frames** are present.

**Key concepts**:
- **Political opportunity structure**: Features of the political environment that encourage or discourage collective action (openness of institutions, stability of elite alignments, presence of elite allies, state repression capacity)
- **Mobilizing structures**: Formal and informal organizational networks through which people are recruited
- **Framing processes**: How movements construct collective action frames that diagnose a problem, assign blame, and propose solutions
- **Cycles of contention**: Waves of collective action that diffuse across groups

**Best for**: Social movements, political mobilization, protests, policy change, union organizing

**Hypothesis language**:
- "We hypothesize that [political opportunity] increases the probability of [mobilization/protest/organization], because it reduces the perceived cost of collective action and increases the expected payoff."
- "We expect that [network density / organizational infrastructure] moderates the [opportunity → mobilization] relationship: opportunities produce more mobilization where [mobilizing structures] are stronger."

**Key papers**: McAdam (1982 *Political Process*); Tarrow (1994 *Power in Movement*); McAdam, McCarthy & Zald (1996)

---

### Resource Mobilization Theory (McCarthy and Zald 1977)

**Core claim**: Social movements are not spontaneous responses to grievances (grievances are ubiquitous) but organized efforts that require **resources** (money, labor, legitimacy) and **organizations** to coordinate collective action.

**Key concepts**:
- **Social movement organization (SMO)**: Organizations that claim to represent movement constituencies
- **Movement industry**: The set of SMOs competing for resources in a social movement sector
- **Resource aggregation**: How SMOs attract money, personnel, and support

**Best for**: Union formation, nonprofit sector, advocacy organizations, movement success/failure

**Hypothesis language**:
- "Resource availability, not grievance intensity, should predict [movement formation/success], because [mobilization requires] coordinating organizations and material support."

**Key paper**: McCarthy & Zald (1977 American Journal of Sociology)

---

## Signaling and Credentialism

### Signaling Theory (Spence 1973)

**Core claim**: In labor markets with information asymmetry, workers invest in **signals** (credentials, certifications) that convey their unobservable quality to employers. Signals are valuable because high-ability workers can acquire them at lower cost than low-ability workers.

**Key concepts**:
- **Separating equilibrium**: High-ability workers signal via credentials; low-ability workers do not — signals successfully separate types
- **Pooling equilibrium**: Credential inflation erodes the separating equilibrium; everyone obtains the credential, reducing its signal value
- **Signaling vs. human capital**: A pure signal does not need to increase productivity; it merely reveals pre-existing quality

**Best for**: Education returns, credentialism, labor market sorting, racial/ethnic differences in credential returns, professional licensing

**Hypothesis language**:
- "If credentials function as signals of pre-existing ability rather than human capital investments, we expect credential returns to [decline over time as they diffuse; differ by group membership; be mediated by employer perceptions rather than skill tests]."
- "In a signaling framework, [Group A] faces a [disadvantage]: even with identical credentials, employers discount the signal because [mechanism], predicting lower returns for [Group A] than [Group B]."

**Key paper**: Spence (1973 Quarterly Journal of Economics)

---

### Credentialism (Collins 1979)

**Core claim**: Educational credentials are used not primarily to train workers or signal ability, but to restrict access to high-status occupations through **cultural gatekeeping** and **occupational monopoly**.

**Mechanisms**: Credential inflation, occupational licensing, "appropriate cultural style" requirements alongside formal credentials

**Key paper**: Collins (1979 *The Credential Society*)

---

## Rational Choice and Social Exchange

### Rational Choice / Coleman's Macro-Social Theory (Coleman 1990; Elster 1989)

**Core claim**: Social institutions and macro outcomes are explained by aggregating individual rational choices, where individuals maximize utility subject to constraints. Coleman's version emphasizes that actors also exchange rights over actions to others (social capital as corporate actors).

**Key concepts**:
- **Purposive action**: Actors have goals; they choose actions that best achieve those goals given constraints
- **Norms**: Externalities of action → actors who benefit from norms (but not their origin) have an interest in enforcing them
- **Trust as rational bet**: Trust is a rational response when the potential gain exceeds the expected loss
- **Corporate actors vs. natural actors**: Organizations are corporate actors with distinct interests from the individuals composing them

**Best for**: Fertility decisions, crime, norm enforcement, public goods problems, organizational behavior

**Hypothesis language**:
- "We predict that actors with lower opportunity costs for [action X] will be more likely to [engage in X], consistent with a rational choice framework in which [action] trades off against [alternatives]."
- "Under conditions of high monitoring / low exit options, norms against [behavior] will be more effectively enforced, reducing [outcome Y]."

---

### Social Exchange Theory (Blau 1964; Homans 1958)

**Core claim**: Social relationships are maintained through an exchange of valued resources; relationships persist when exchanges are balanced or partners perceive them as equitable.

**Key concepts**: **Distributive justice**: Rewards proportional to investments. **Power-dependence**: Power is the inverse of dependence on a relationship.

**Best for**: Organizational behavior, friendship formation, intimate partner relationships, authority and compliance

---

## Intergroup Relations

### Contact Hypothesis (Allport 1954; Pettigrew and Tropp 2006)

**Core claim**: Direct intergroup contact under conditions of **equal status, cooperative interdependence, institutional support, and acquaintance potential** reduces prejudice and improves intergroup attitudes.

**Conditions required for effective contact**:
1. Equal status between groups in the contact situation
2. Common goals / cooperative task structure
3. Intergroup cooperation (not competition)
4. Authority sanction / institutional support

**Modern extensions**:
- **Extended contact** (Wright et al. 1997): Knowing an ingroup member who has an outgroup friend improves attitudes
- **Parasocial contact**: Media exposure to outgroup members reduces prejudice under certain conditions

**Best for**: Race relations, immigration attitudes, desegregation, diversity interventions, political polarization

**Hypothesis language**:
- "Under [high-quality / low-quality] contact conditions, we expect [more / less] attitude change, because [condition X] [is / is not] present, predicting [higher / lower] prejudice reduction."
- "We hypothesize that the effect of [contact] on [attitude] is moderated by [institutional support], consistent with Allport's (1954) conditions for effective contact."

**Key papers**: Allport (1954 *The Nature of Prejudice*); Pettigrew & Tropp (2006 Journal of Personality and Social Psychology meta-analysis)

---

### Labeling Theory / Deviance (Becker 1963; Lemert 1967)

**Core claim**: Deviance is not an inherent property of acts but a label applied through social processes. **Primary deviance** is the initial rule violation; **secondary deviance** is the deviant career that emerges after the label is applied and the actor internalizes the deviant identity.

**Key concepts**: **Deviant career**: Stages of incorporation into a deviant role. **Moral entrepreneurs**: Actors who campaign to create or enforce rules. **Stigma** (Goffman 1963): A deeply discrediting attribute that reduces the bearer to a "spoiled identity."

**Best for**: Criminal justice, incarceration effects, mental health labeling, disability, substance use, educational tracking

**Hypothesis language**:
- "We hypothesize that formal [arrest / diagnosis / tracking] labels increase the probability of [future deviance / continued labeling] through identity transformation, not because of the underlying behavior."
- "Following labeling theory, we expect the effect of [label] on [outcome] to be stronger for [groups who lack resources to contest the label]."

### Organizations and Institutions

**Neo-Institutional Theory** (Meyer & Rowan 1977; DiMaggio & Powell 1983)
- Core: Organizations adopt structures and practices not because they are efficient, but because they are culturally legitimate (institutional myths)
- Three isomorphism mechanisms: coercive (regulatory pressure), mimetic (uncertainty → copy successful orgs), normative (professionalization)
- Hypothesis template: "Organizations in fields with [stronger regulatory pressure / greater uncertainty / more professionalized workforces] are more likely to adopt [practice X], even when [practice X] does not improve [efficiency/outcomes]."
- Key concepts: Institutional logics, organizational fields, decoupling (formal adoption without substantive change), ceremonial conformity

**Organizational Ecology** (Hannan & Freeman 1977, 1989)
- Core: Organizations face structural inertia; selection (founding/failure) rather than adaptation drives population-level change
- Key mechanisms: density dependence (legitimation at low density, competition at high density), resource partitioning, age dependence (liability of newness/adolescence/obsolescence)
- Hypothesis template: "The founding rate of [organization type] increases with [population density] at low levels (legitimation) but decreases at high levels (competition)."

**Practice Theory** (Bourdieu 1977; Giddens 1984; Schatzki 2002)
- Core: Social life is organized around routinized practices that integrate bodily activities, material arrangements, and shared meanings
- Key concepts: Habitus (durable dispositions), field (structured social space), capital (economic, cultural, social, symbolic), duality of structure (structures both constrain and enable action)
- Hypothesis template: "Individuals with [type of capital] are more likely to engage in [practice X], and this relationship is [stronger/weaker] in [field Y] due to [field-specific logic]."

---

### Critical and Contemporary Theory

**Feminist Epistemology / Standpoint Theory** (Harding 1986; Smith 1987; Collins 2000; Haraway 1988)
- Core: Knowledge is socially situated; marginalized standpoints offer epistemic privilege (seeing what dominant perspectives miss)
- Key concepts: Situated knowledge, strong objectivity, intersectionality as methodology, relations of ruling
- Application: Justifies centering marginalized experiences; challenges claims to universal knowledge

**Labor Process Theory** (Braverman 1974; Burawoy 1979; Edwards 1979)
- Core: Capitalist production requires control over the labor process; workers resist; management develops new control strategies
- Key mechanisms: Deskilling, Taylorization, consent/coercion dialectic, frontier of control
- Hypothesis template: "Introduction of [technology/management practice] is associated with [deskilling/reskilling] of [worker group], mediated by [management control strategy]."

**Actor-Network Theory (ANT)** (Latour 2005; Callon 1986; Law 1992)
- Core: Social phenomena are produced by heterogeneous networks of human and non-human actors (actants)
- Key concepts: Translation, enrollment, obligatory passage points, black-boxing
- Application: Trace how technologies, institutions, and ideas are stabilized through network building

### Intersectionality

**Foundational works**: Crenshaw (1989, 1991); Collins (1990); Collins and Bilge (2016)

**Core claim**: Race, gender, class, sexuality, and other axes of inequality are not separate, additive systems but mutually constitutive — they interact to produce distinct experiences that cannot be understood by examining any axis in isolation.

**Key concepts**:
- **Matrix of domination** (Collins 1990): Interlocking systems of oppression organized through structural, disciplinary, hegemonic, and interpersonal domains of power
- **Intersecting identities**: Position in multiple hierarchies simultaneously creates unique structural locations (e.g., Black women face neither "race discrimination" nor "sex discrimination" in isolation)
- **Privilege and oppression as simultaneous**: Individuals can be simultaneously privileged on one axis and oppressed on another; effects do not simply cancel

**Research types**:
1. **Group-centered**: Compare outcomes for the intersection group vs. comparison groups (e.g., Black women vs. White women vs. Black men vs. White men)
2. **Process-centered**: Trace how axes of inequality shape access to resources, experiences of institutions, and identity formation
3. **System-centered**: Examine how macro-level structures co-produce race-gender-class hierarchies

**Formal hypothesis specification for intersectionality**:

Intersectionality predicts a **multiplicative** or **unique** effect — not just main effects or their sum:
```
Additive model (NOT intersectional): Y = α + β₁(Black) + β₂(Female) + ε
  → Assumes effects of race and gender are independent and additive

Intersectional model: Y = α + β₁(Black) + β₂(Female) + β₃(Black×Female) + ε
  → β₃ ≠ 0: The combination produces a distinct outcome
  → If β₃ < 0: Disadvantages are compounding (worse than either alone)
  → If β₃ > 0: An advantage unique to the intersection (e.g., community networks)
```

**Hypothesis language for intersectionality**:
- "We hypothesize that the effect of [X] on [Y] differs for [intersection group] in ways that cannot be explained by [race alone] or [gender alone]."
- "We expect [Black women] to face [unique barrier] because [mechanism], predicting a negative interaction between race and gender."
- "We do not simply expect additive penalties; we hypothesize that the intersection of [race and class] creates qualitatively distinct [mechanisms]."

**Data requirements**:
- Large samples with sufficient N in all intersection cells (often a problem; rare intersections require oversampling or pooled data)
- When N is small: use descriptive or process-based approaches; avoid overinterpreting interaction coefficients
- Reporting: Always show all main effects AND interaction; report predicted values / margins at each intersection

**Key papers for empirical intersectionality**:
- Browne and Misra (2003 ASR review), Bettie (2003), Crenshaw (1991 SSRN), Grollman (2014 ASR)

### Global / Non-Western Theoretical Traditions

**Why it matters**: Most canonical sociological theory was produced in Western European and North American contexts. Applying these frameworks globally without interrogation can reproduce analytic blind spots.

#### Coloniality and Decolonial Sociology (Quijano; Mignolo; Grosfoguel)

**Coloniality of power** (Quijano 2000):
- Core claim: Colonial racial classification did not end with formal decolonization; it persists in knowledge production, labor organization, gender systems, and state structures
- "Coloniality" (the logic) vs. "colonialism" (the historical period)
- Best for: Global South inequality, race in Latin America, knowledge hierarchies in academia, migrant labor

**Decolonial critique of social science**:
- Western social science uses Europe as the default/universal; "Southern" experiences become deviation or exception
- Call: Use Southern cases as theory-generating, not just theory-testing, sites

**Key works**: Quijano (2000 Latin American Perspectives), Mignolo (2000 Local Histories/Global Designs), Grosfoguel (2011)

---

#### World-Systems Theory (Wallerstein 1974, 1980; Chase-Dunn)

**Core claim**: The capitalist world-economy creates a hierarchical division between core, semi-periphery, and periphery that structures labor, development, and inequality globally.

**Mechanisms**: Unequal exchange; capital extraction from periphery to core; commodity chains; interstate competition

**Best for**: Global economic inequality, development, migration, comparative historical analysis

**Formal hypothesis**: Countries' positions in world-system (core/semi-periphery/periphery) moderate the relationship between [X] and [Y], such that effects found in core nations do not generalize to peripheral nations.

---

#### Postcolonial Theory (Said; Bhabha; Spivak; Fanon)

**Said's Orientalism** (1978): Western knowledge production constructs "the East" as exotic, irrational, and static — a discursive formation that legitimizes domination.
- Best for: Media framing of non-Western populations, immigration discourse, linguistic othering

**Fanon** (1952, 1961): Colonized subjects internalize colonial racial hierarchies; anti-colonial struggle requires psychological and material decolonization.
- Best for: Racial identity, internalized racism, Black consciousness, immigrant identity

**Bhabha's hybridity and mimicry**: Colonial subjects produce hybrid identities; mimicry (adopting the colonizer's culture imperfectly) is a form of resistance.
- Best for: Postcolonial identity, diaspora, language choice

**Spivak's "Can the Subaltern Speak?"** (1988): Marginalized groups are often constituted as incapable of knowledge production; researchers must reflexively examine whose knowledge counts.
- Best for: Research methodology ethics, voice and representation

---

#### Ubuntu and African Relational Philosophy

**"I am because we are"** (Mbiti; Tutu; Metz 2011):
- Ubuntu: Human personhood is constituted through communal relations, not individualism
- Core claim: Well-being, identity, and ethics are fundamentally relational and communal — not an individual property
- Best for: Community-level analyses in Sub-Saharan Africa, collective action, care labor, family support networks
- Contrast with: Western individualism in rational choice models; Putnam's social capital (which treats community as instrumental to individual outcomes)

**Hypothesis language**: "Consistent with Ubuntu philosophy, we hypothesize that [community cohesion / collective obligations] directly shapes [outcomes], independent of individual-level resources."

#### Confucian Social Theory and East Asian Sociology

**Key concepts**:
- **Guanxi** (关系): Personal relationship networks that function as social capital with strong obligations; differs from Burt/Granovetter in that guanxi embeds moral obligations and reciprocity beyond information brokerage
- **Face** (Mianzi / Lian): Reputational currency that operates through public recognition and shame; structures status hierarchy and institutional behavior
- **Filial piety** (Xiao): Obligations to parents/ancestors; shapes intergenerational transfers, elder care, fertility decisions
- **Li** (礼): Propriety, ritual, norms; Confucian equivalent of institutional rules

**Best for**: China, Taiwan, Korea, Japan, Chinese diaspora; education decisions, labor market, family obligations, organizational behavior

**Theoretical note**: Guanxi ≠ Western social capital (Lin 2001). Guanxi involves particularistic exchange, not generalized trust; should not be treated as equivalent.

#### Southern Theory (Connell 2007; Bhambra 2014)

**Core claim**: Global social science is organized around Northern/Western intellectual centers that extract knowledge from the periphery without reciprocal theoretical integration. "Southern theory" calls for theory-building FROM the global South.

**Implications for research**:
- When applying canonical theories to Southern contexts, treat mismatch as analytically interesting, not as deviation from the universal
- Report when well-established Western findings do NOT replicate in other contexts as a positive contribution
- Cite Southern scholars (not just Western scholars writing about the South)

### Integrating Non-Western Frameworks with Canonical Sociology

| Non-Western tradition | Best combined with | Research context |
|----------------------|--------------------|-----------------|
| Coloniality | Stratification, social closure | Race, labor, global inequality |
| World-systems | Organizational sociology | Development, multinational firms |
| Postcolonial | Symbolic boundaries, framing | Immigration discourse, media |
| Ubuntu | Social capital, collective action | Sub-Saharan Africa, diaspora |
| Guanxi/Confucian | Network theory, status attainment | East Asia, Chinese diaspora |
| Intersectionality | Status attainment, segmented assimilation | Race, gender, class in any context |

**Writing tip**: When using non-Western frameworks in a paper targeting Western journals (ASR, AJS), explicitly name the tradition, explain its core claims in 2–3 sentences as you would for any theory, and then derive testable predictions. Reviewers unfamiliar with the tradition need this context.

## Combining Multiple Frameworks

Strong papers often integrate 2 frameworks:
- **Primary framework**: Main causal mechanism
- **Secondary framework**: Moderating condition or boundary specification

Example: "We draw on social capital theory (Lin 2001) to explain the main effect, while incorporating insights from segmented assimilation (Portes and Zhou 1993) to account for heterogeneity across ethnic communities."

**Intersectionality + canonical stratification**:
"We draw on Crenshaw's (1989) intersectionality framework to specify how race and gender jointly shape returns to education, predicting that the wage gap between Black and White workers will be larger for women than men (a negative interaction). This prediction extends status attainment theory (Blau and Duncan 1967) by replacing its additive assumption with an intersectional one."

**Non-Western + canonical**:
"We draw on guanxi theory (Bian 1997) rather than structural holes (Burt 1992) to theorize the mechanism, because guanxi captures the moral obligation embedded in Chinese network ties that Western brokerage models treat as absent."

## Hypothesis Language Templates by Theory

Quick reference: verbatim sentence starters for deriving formal hypotheses from common theories.

### Stratification / Inequality

**Cultural capital**: "Because [group X] possesses [lower/higher] cultural capital, we expect them to receive [less/more favorable] evaluations in [institutional context], predicting [lower/higher] [outcome Y]."

**Social closure**: "We hypothesize that [group A]'s monopoly on [credential/characteristic] restricts [group B]'s access to [valued resource], predicting [lower] [outcome] for [group B] even after controlling for [productivity-related attributes]."

**Cumulative advantage**: "Early [advantage X] compounds across [career stages / years / waves], such that the [outcome gap] between [groups] grows over time, predicting a positive interaction between [X] and [time]."

**Status characteristics**: "We hypothesize that [race/gender/credential] operates as a diffuse status cue in [evaluative context], leading [evaluators] to assign lower performance expectations to [lower-status group], predicting [lower] [evaluations/opportunities/wages] independent of [task-relevant attributes]."

**Categorical inequality**: "Because [organizational practice] relies on [categorical distinction race/gender/nativity], we expect [group A] and [group B] to differ in [outcome Y] through [exploitation/opportunity hoarding], even net of individual-level [attributes]."

**Signaling**: "If [credential] functions primarily as a signal rather than a human capital investment, its return should be [higher/lower/zero] when [employer can directly observe ability], consistent with Spence's (1973) prediction that signals lose value when direct observation is available."

---

### Networks / Social Capital

**Strength of weak ties**: "Individuals with [more/fewer] weak ties will access [more/less] non-redundant information, predicting [higher/lower] [job-finding success / information diffusion / social integration]."

**Structural holes**: "Bridging structural holes between [groups] confers [informational/positional] advantages, predicting that [actors who bridge] will have [higher] [career outcomes / innovation / earnings] than those embedded in closed networks."

**Social capital (Lin)**: "Resources accessed through [higher-status / more diverse] network ties should produce [higher] returns to [action X], because [mechanism: better information, sponsorship, access to resources]."

---

### Culture and Cognition

**Frames/schemas**: "We hypothesize that exposure to [Frame F] activates [Schema S], which in turn predicts [cognition/attitude/behavior Y], consistent with Goffman's (1974) framing theory."

**Institutional logics**: "Actors embedded in [Market/Family/Religious] institutional logic will [respond to] [situation X] differently from those in [alternative logic], predicting [behavioral difference] because [each logic prescribes distinct norms and values]."

**Symbolic boundaries**: "We predict that [group A] will draw [stronger/weaker] moral and cultural boundaries against [group B] when [structural threat/proximity condition], because boundary work intensifies when [in-group identity is threatened]."

---

### Life Course and Demography

**Life course**: "Events occurring during [sensitive period / early stage] of the life course will have [larger/persistent] effects on [outcome Y], because [timing mechanism: early investments compound; late events cannot compensate], consistent with the life course principle of [timing/linked lives]."

**Cumulative disadvantage**: "Each [disadvantage/setback X] increases the probability of [subsequent disadvantage Y], producing an accelerating gap between [advantaged and disadvantaged groups] over [career/life span], predicting a [positive and growing] interaction between [X] and [time]."

---

### Immigration and Assimilation

**Segmented assimilation**: "Second-generation [group]'s assimilation trajectory will follow [upward/downward/selective acculturation] path as a function of [co-ethnic community strength / structural reception / family context], rather than a single straight-line trajectory."

**New assimilation**: "Boundary [blurring/crossing/shifting] between [immigrant group] and [mainstream] should increase with [generational duration / intermarriage / spatial integration], predicting [declining ethnic identification / rising socioeconomic parity] across generations."

---

### Social Movements

**Political opportunity**: "We hypothesize that [opportunity condition: elite division, weakened state, presence of allies] is positively associated with [movement emergence/success], because [expanded opportunities reduce the cost and increase the expected payoff of collective action]."

**Resource mobilization**: "We predict that [organizational resource endowment] predicts [mobilization capacity/success] more strongly than [grievance intensity], because [resources, not grievances, are the proximate constraint on collective action]."

---

### Race and Discrimination

**Racial formation**: "We hypothesize that [policy/practice X] functions as a racial project, reproducing [racial disparity] by [mechanism], even without explicit racial intent, consistent with Omi and Winant's (1994) theory that racial projects link representation to resource distribution."

**Contact hypothesis**: "Consistent with Allport's (1954) contact hypothesis, we predict that [contact] reduces [prejudice/discrimination] only when [conditions: equal status, cooperative goals, institutional support] are met, predicting a [moderation] by [contact quality]."

**Labeling theory**: "Following Becker (1963), we hypothesize that [formal label X] — not the underlying behavior — predicts [secondary deviance], because the label triggers [identity transformation, social exclusion, self-fulfilling expectations] that produce [continued involvement in labeled behavior]."

---

### Analytical Sociology Mechanism Language

**Coleman's boat**:
"We argue that [macro condition X] shapes [outcome Y] through a three-step mechanism: [macro condition] → [situational mechanism: how X changes actors' situations/beliefs/resources] → [action mechanism: what actors do given their situation] → [aggregation: how actions sum to Y]. This micro-founded explanation distinguishes our theory from [macro-level alternative] that cannot account for [scope condition or variation by group]."

**DBO**:
"The mechanism operates at the individual level through actors' desires ([D]), beliefs ([B]), and opportunities ([O]): [D: what actors want in this context]; [B: what they believe about their options]; [O: what options are structurally available]; together these produce [A: action], which aggregates to [macro outcome Y]."

**Elster mechanism classification**:
"We classify this as a [cognitive/motivational/interactional/institutional/material] mechanism: [X] affects actors' [beliefs/incentives/interactions/rules/resources], which changes [action A], producing [outcome Y]."
