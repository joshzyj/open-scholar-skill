## 1) How to Use This Reference

Use this file after the user provides a broad idea.

Workflow:
1. Pick the closest domain pattern from Section 3.
2. Draft 2-3 formal RQs with formulas in Section 2.
3. Attach 1-2 mechanisms from Section 4.
4. Match each RQ to feasible data/design options in Section 5.
5. Run novelty and risk checks in Sections 6 and 7.

## 2) RQ Formula Library

Use these sentence forms directly.

- Causal-mechanism form:
  `Among [population] in [context/time], how does [X] affect [Y], and through what mechanism(s) [M]?`

- Comparative form:
  `How and why does the [X]->[Y] relationship differ across [group A] and [group B] in [context/time]?`

- Policy-evaluation form:
  `What is the effect of [policy/treatment X] on [Y] among [target population], relative to [counterfactual]?`

- Organizational form:
  `How do organizational features [X] shape [Y] for [workers/clients/members] under [conditions]?`

- Spatial form:
  `How do place-based conditions [X] influence [Y] across [neighborhoods/regions], and for whom?`

- Temporal-change form:
  `How has the relationship between [X] and [Y] changed from [t1] to [t2], and what explains that change?`

## 3) Domain Pattern Bank

### A. Inequality and Mobility

- Typical Y: wages, wealth, educational attainment, health, incarceration exposure
- Typical X: class origin, neighborhood deprivation, labor market precarity, school quality
- Mechanisms: resource access, institutional gatekeeping, network closure, stress burden
- Strong RQ starter:
  `Among [population], how does [neighborhood/school/workplace stratification] shape [mobility/health/outcome], net of family background?`

### B. Migration and Integration

- Typical Y: labor market outcomes, language adoption, civic participation, belonging
- Typical X: legal status, destination context, discrimination, coethnic networks
- Mechanisms: selective exposure, information frictions, credential recognition, social incorporation
- Strong RQ starter:
  `Among [immigrant generation/group], how does [legal/institutional context] affect [integration outcome], via [mechanism]?`

### C. Health and Social Determinants

- Typical Y: morbidity, mental health, preventive care use, mortality risk
- Typical X: segregation, pollution, healthcare access, employment instability
- Mechanisms: chronic stress, material deprivation, care access pathways, behavioral adaptation
- Strong RQ starter:
  `How do social and spatial exposures [X] influence [health outcome Y] among [population], and through which pathways?`

### D. Political Behavior and Public Opinion

- Typical Y: turnout, protest, trust, polarization, policy attitudes
- Typical X: media exposure, network composition, repression, local shocks
- Mechanisms: information diffusion, identity activation, grievance framing, perceived efficacy
- Strong RQ starter:
  `How does [information/social exposure X] affect [political outcome Y] across [groups/places], and when is the effect strongest?`

### E. Organizations, Work, and Technology

- Typical Y: hiring outcomes, promotion, productivity, burnout, turnover
- Typical X: algorithmic management, remote/hybrid policy, monitoring intensity, team structure
- Mechanisms: autonomy, evaluation transparency, coordination costs, bias amplification
- Strong RQ starter:
  `How does [organizational/technology practice X] shape [worker outcome Y], and for which workers does the effect differ?`

### F. Family, Gender, and Life Course

- Typical Y: fertility, union transitions, caregiving burden, earnings penalties
- Typical X: policy context, household bargaining, labor flexibility, gender norms
- Mechanisms: opportunity cost, social norms, time scarcity, institutional supports
- Strong RQ starter:
  `How does [institutional/family context X] influence [life-course outcome Y], and how does this vary by gender/class/race?`

### G. Education and Stratification

- Typical Y: achievement, college entry, major choice, persistence, debt burden
- Typical X: tracking, school resources, teacher expectations, peer composition
- Mechanisms: cumulative advantage, labeling, social capital, institutional sorting
- Strong RQ starter:
  `How do school organizational conditions [X] shape [educational outcome Y] among [student population]?`

### H. Crime, Policing, and Legal Institutions

- Typical Y: arrest risk, sentencing severity, recidivism, legal trust
- Typical X: enforcement intensity, neighborhood surveillance, policy reforms
- Mechanisms: deterrence, selective enforcement, legal cynicism, procedural justice
- Strong RQ starter:
  `What is the impact of [justice system practice/policy X] on [legal outcome Y], and does this effect differ across groups?`

### I. Language, Culture, and Identity

- Typical Y: linguistic variation, code-switching, status evaluations, identity expression
- Typical X: interaction setting, audience composition, platform norms
- Mechanisms: indexicality, audience design, prestige/stigma, boundary work
- Strong RQ starter:
  `How does [social context X] condition [linguistic/cultural practice Y], and what identities are being negotiated?`

### J. Race, Ethnicity, and Racial Inequality

ASA sections: Racial and Ethnic Minorities; Race, Gender, and Class; Latina/o Sociology; Asia and Asian America; Sociology of Indigenous Peoples and Native Nations

- Typical Y: residential segregation, racial wealth gap, health disparities, hiring discrimination, criminal justice exposure, educational achievement gaps, deportation risk
- Typical X: racial classification, skin tone, neighborhood racial composition, discrimination exposure, immigration enforcement, historical exclusion policies, affirmative action
- Mechanisms: structural racism, cumulative disadvantage, residential sorting, statistical discrimination, stereotype threat, social closure, colorism, settler colonialism
- Strong RQ starter:
  `How does [racial/ethnic stratification process X] shape [outcome Y] among [racial/ethnic group] in [context], and through what institutional mechanism?`
- Heterogeneity note: decompose by nativity (immigrant vs. native-born), generation (1st/2nd/3rd+), skin tone, gender, and class where sample permits

### K. Urban, Community, and Neighborhood Sociology

ASA sections: Community and Urban Sociology

- Strong RQ starter:
  `How do place-based conditions [X] shape [residential/community outcome Y] across [neighborhoods/cities], and for which residents are effects strongest?`

### M. Environmental and Climate Sociology

- Typical X: industrial facility siting, race/class neighborhood composition, carbon pricing, climate communication, disaster experience, corporate practice

  `How does [technology/institutional practice X] shape [social outcome Y] across [groups/organizations], and through what sociotechnical mechanism?`

- Strong RQ starter:
  `How does [status/interactional/situational context X] shape [micro-level behavioral or emotional outcome Y] among [population/group], and through what social-psychological mechanism?`

## 4) Mechanism Menus

Use one primary and one secondary mechanism per RQ.

- Resource channel: access to money, time, information, institutional support
- Network channel: exposure, diffusion, brokerage, closure, homophily
- Institutional channel: rules, gatekeeping, sanctions, administrative burden
- Cultural channel: meanings, norms, stigma, legitimacy
- Psychological channel: stress, efficacy, belonging, threat perception
- Spatial channel: distance decay, segregation, environmental constraints
- Temporal channel: sequencing, path dependence, cumulative disadvantage

Mechanism statement template:
`We expect [X] to influence [Y] primarily through [mechanism], because [short causal logic].`

## 5) Data and Design Matching

Use this quick table to avoid infeasible RQs.

- Strong causal policy question -> administrative panel + DiD/event study
- Treatment threshold question -> RD with clear assignment cutoff
- Individual change question -> panel FE models
- Diffusion/network question -> relational data + network models
- Text/meaning question -> corpus/interview/discourse + NLP/qual coding
- Mechanism-rich but low N question -> mixed methods (quant + interviews)

Feasibility flags:
- If key X has weak measurement -> downgrade to exploratory/associational framing.
- If confounders are mostly unobserved -> propose IV, natural experiment, or sensitivity bounds.
- If timeline is short -> prefer secondary data with known variable quality.

## 6) Literature Scan Query Templates

Use these query templates in Step 3 (Quick Literature Scan). Adapt bracketed terms to the specific topic.

### Primary Search Engines
- **Google Scholar**: `scholar.google.com` — search titles, abstracts, full text
- **Semantic Scholar**: `semanticscholar.org` — AI-enhanced relevance, citation graphs
- **JSTOR**: `jstor.org` — sociology, demography, linguistics journals
- **Sociological Abstracts** (via ProQuest): authoritative sociology index

### Query Templates

**Topic + Outcome query:**
`"[main topic]" "[outcome variable]" sociology OR demography`

**Mechanism query:**
`"[proposed mechanism]" "[outcome]" "[population group]"`

**Identification / methods query:**
`"[topic]" "panel data" OR "natural experiment" OR "difference-in-differences" OR "fixed effects"`

**Recency filter (recent 10 years):**
`"[topic]" "[outcome]" after:2014`

**Review / synthesis query:**
`"[topic]" "systematic review" OR "meta-analysis" OR "review article"`

**Cross-national / comparative:**
`"[topic]" "[outcome]" "cross-national" OR "comparative" OR "OECD"`

**Closest competitor search:**
`"[X] effect on [Y]" "[target population]" [journal: American Sociological Review OR AJS OR Demography OR Social Forces]`

### Novelty Checks

Rate each candidate RQ on four dimensions (High/Medium/Low):

1. **Theoretical increment:**
   - Does it adjudicate between competing explanations?
   - Does it connect literatures that rarely talk to each other?

2. **Empirical increment:**
   - New data, new context, new time period, or sharper measurement than prior work?

3. **Identification increment:**
   - Better causal leverage than prior studies (natural experiment, new instrument, panel)?

4. **Substantive importance:**
   - Would the answer matter for core social science debates or policy?

**Novelty threat classifications (assign after running searches):**
- `SATURATED` — 3+ high-quality studies answer this exact question → pivot required
- `INCREMENTAL` — prior work exists, but gap in population/time/method → justify extension explicitly
- `GAP` — question raised but not well-answered → strong entry point
- `UNEXPLORED` — question not directly studied → high-risk, high-reward; check whether the gap reflects difficulty rather than neglect

If all four dimensions are Medium/Low, revise the RQ before proceeding.

## 7) Common Failure Modes

- Overbroad Y: "inequality" without specific measurable outcome.
- Mechanism-free question: asks whether, not how or why.
- Data mismatch: asks causal question with only one weak cross-section.
- Scope ambiguity: no population, context, or time boundary.
- Trivial extension: adds one control variable but no new theoretical leverage.
- Assumed novelty: claims "this has not been studied" without running a search.
- Data blindness: proposes RQ without knowing if key variables exist in any accessible dataset.

Fix pattern:
1. Narrow Y.
2. Add mechanism.
3. Declare scope.
4. Match design to claim strength.
5. Run literature scan before claiming novelty.
6. Confirm key variables exist in a named, accessible dataset.
