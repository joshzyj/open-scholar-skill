# Theoretical Frameworks for Social Science Research

## Core Sociological Theories

### Stratification and Inequality

**Status Attainment Model** (Blau and Duncan 1967; Sewell, Haller, Portes 1969)
- Core claim: Occupational attainment is a function of background characteristics and educational attainment
- Mechanisms: Credential signaling, skill acquisition, social influence from significant others
- Best for: Intergenerational mobility, education-earnings linkage
- Key papers: Blau & Duncan (1967 AJS); Sewell et al. (1969 AJS)

**Cultural Capital** (Bourdieu 1984, 1986)
- Core claim: Cultural knowledge, skills, and dispositions are unequally distributed and converted into educational and economic advantage
- Three forms: Embodied, objectified, institutionalized
- Mechanisms: Institutional matching (elite gatekeepers favor elite cultural tastes), transmission through family
- Best for: Education inequality, occupational sorting, class reproduction
- Key papers: Bourdieu (1984 Distinction); DiMaggio (1982 ASR)

**Social Closure** (Weber; Parkin 1979; Murphy 1988)
- Core claim: Dominant groups monopolize valued resources by excluding outsiders through credentials, licenses, or group membership
- Mechanisms: Credential inflation, occupational licensing, informal exclusion
- Best for: Professions, racial/ethnic economic inequality, credentialism

**Cumulative Advantage / Matthew Effect** (DiPrete and Eirich 2006; Merton 1968)
- Core claim: Early advantages compound over time; inequality grows through self-reinforcing processes
- Best for: Life course inequality, educational careers, career mobility

### Social Networks and Capital

**Strength of Weak Ties** (Granovetter 1973)
- Core claim: Weak ties (acquaintances) bridge otherwise disconnected network clusters and provide access to non-redundant information
- Mechanisms: Information brokerage, bridging social capital
- Best for: Job search, information diffusion, social integration
- Key paper: Granovetter (1973 AJS)

**Structural Holes** (Burt 1992)
- Core claim: Individuals who bridge disconnected groups gain informational and positional advantages
- Mechanisms: Information brokerage, control over resource flows
- Best for: Organizational advantage, career success, innovation
- Key paper: Burt (1992 Structural Holes)

**Social Capital** (Lin 2001; Coleman 1988; Putnam 2000)
- Lin: Resources embedded in social networks that can be mobilized through ties
- Coleman: Trust, norms, network closure that facilitate collective action
- Putnam: Bonding vs. bridging social capital at community level
- Best for: Mobility, community resilience, civic participation

**Network Diffusion** (Centola and Macy 2007; Watts 1999)
- Core claim: Information and behaviors spread through network structures; complex behaviors require wide bridges, not just bridges
- Best for: Health behavior diffusion, social movements, technology adoption

---

### Cultural and Meaning-Making Theories

**Symbolic Boundaries** (Lamont 1992; Lamont and Molnár 2002)
- Core claim: Groups construct moral and cultural boundaries to define who belongs and who doesn't
- Types: Moral, socioeconomic, cultural boundaries
- Best for: Intergroup relations, racial attitudes, national identity
- Key paper: Lamont (1992 Money, Morals, and Manners)

**Frames and Schemas** (Goffman 1974; DiMaggio 1997; Vaisey 2009)
- Goffman: Frames organize experience and interpretation
- DiMaggio: Schemas are cognitive structures activated by contexts
- Vaisey: Dual-process; moral schemas motivate action, discursive frames justify post-hoc
- Best for: Survey response processes, media framing, moral reasoning

**Institutional Logics** (Friedland and Alford 1991; Thornton et al. 2012)
- Core claim: Different institutional orders (market, religion, family, state) provide distinct values, goals, and practices
- Best for: Organizational behavior, work-family conflict, professional norms

---

### Immigration and Assimilation

**Straight-Line Assimilation** (Gordon 1964)
- Core claim: Immigrants gradually assimilate into mainstream society across generations
- Stages: Cultural → structural → marital → identificational assimilation
- Best for: Historical immigration, European immigrants to US

**Segmented Assimilation** (Portes and Zhou 1993; Portes and Rumbaut 2001)
- Core claim: Second-generation immigrants assimilate into different segments of society depending on co-ethnic community, family context, and structural reception
- Three paths: Upward assimilation, downward assimilation, selective acculturation
- Best for: Contemporary immigration, racial/ethnic minorities, second generation

**New Assimilation Theory** (Alba and Nee 2003)
- Core claim: Boundaries between immigrant groups and mainstream shift over time through institutional mechanisms
- Mechanisms: Boundary blurring, boundary shifting, boundary crossing
- Best for: Contemporary assimilation, racial/ethnic identity change

**Transnationalism** (Glick Schiller et al. 1992; Portes et al. 1999)
- Core claim: Immigrants maintain sustained connections to origin country while settling in destination
- Best for: Remittances, identity, political mobilization, return migration

---

### Linguistic and Language Theories

**Linguistic Capital** (Bourdieu 1991)
- Core claim: Languages and language varieties have differential market value; dominant language = linguistic capital
- Mechanisms: Legitimate language, symbolic power, field-specific exchange
- Best for: Language and inequality, accent discrimination, educational language policies

**Language Ideologies** (Woolard 1998; Irvine and Gal 2000)
- Core claim: Beliefs about language structure social hierarchies and justify language policies
- Best for: Language policy, bilingualism attitudes, language discrimination

**Accommodation Theory** (Giles 1973; Giles and Powesland 1975)
- Core claim: Speakers adjust their speech style (convergence/divergence) to signal social identity and manage impressions
- Best for: Accent modification, code-switching, intergroup contact

**Language Shift and Maintenance** (Fishman 1991; Grenoble and Whaley 2006)
- Core claim: Language shift occurs when speakers shift to a dominant language across generations; maintenance requires active community support
- Best for: Heritage languages, minority language vitality, bilingual education

---

### Life Course Theory

**Life Course Perspective** (Elder 1994; Elder and Giele 2009)
- Five principles: Historical time and place, timing, linked lives, human agency, life span development
- Best for: Longitudinal analysis, cohort effects, biographical approaches

**Cumulative Disadvantage** (DiPrete and Eirich 2006)
- Disadvantages compound; each setback increases probability of future setbacks
- Best for: Health disparities, educational careers, incarceration effects

---

### Computational and Quantitative Theory

**Structural Equation Modeling Framework**: Mediation and moderation as causal structures
**Social influence models**: Peer effects, network endogeneity
**Bayesian updating**: How individuals revise beliefs in response to information
**Matching and sorting models**: Labor market matching, educational sorting

## Choosing the Right Framework

| Research Question | Recommended Theory |
|-------------------|--------------------|
| Why do children of poor families stay poor? | Cultural capital, social closure, status attainment |
| How do people find jobs? | Strength of weak ties, structural holes, social capital |
| Why do immigrants assimilate differently? | Segmented assimilation, new assimilation, transnationalism |
| Why does language change across generations? | Language shift, linguistic capital, accommodation theory |
| How do inequalities grow over time? | Cumulative advantage, life course |
| Why do groups differ in attitudes/values? | Frames/schemas, institutional logics, symbolic boundaries |

### Intersectionality

**Foundational works**: Crenshaw (1989, 1991); Collins (1990); Collins and Bilge (2016)

**Core claim**: Race, gender, class, sexuality, and other axes of inequality are not separate, additive systems but mutually constitutive — they interact to produce distinct experiences that cannot be understood by examining any axis in isolation.

**Key concepts**:
- **Matrix of domination** (Collins 1990): Interlocking systems of oppression organized through structural, disciplinary, hegemonic, and interpersonal domains of power
- **Intersecting identities**: Position in multiple hierarchies simultaneously creates unique structural locations (e.g., Black women face neither "race discrimination" nor "sex discrimination" in isolation)
- **Privilege and oppression as simultaneous**: Individuals can be simultaneously privileged on one axis and oppressed on another; effects do not simply cancel

**Research types**:
1. **Group-centered**: Compare outcomes for the intersection group vs. comparison groups (e.g., Black women vs. White women vs. Black men vs. White men)
2. **Process-centered**: Trace how axes of inequality shape access to resources, experiences of institutions, and identity formation
3. **System-centered**: Examine how macro-level structures co-produce race-gender-class hierarchies

**Formal hypothesis specification for intersectionality**:

Intersectionality predicts a **multiplicative** or **unique** effect — not just main effects or their sum:
```
Additive model (NOT intersectional): Y = α + β₁(Black) + β₂(Female) + ε
  → Assumes effects of race and gender are independent and additive

Intersectional model: Y = α + β₁(Black) + β₂(Female) + β₃(Black×Female) + ε
  → β₃ ≠ 0: The combination produces a distinct outcome
  → If β₃ < 0: Disadvantages are compounding (worse than either alone)
  → If β₃ > 0: An advantage unique to the intersection (e.g., community networks)
```

**Hypothesis language for intersectionality**:
- "We hypothesize that the effect of [X] on [Y] differs for [intersection group] in ways that cannot be explained by [race alone] or [gender alone]."
- "We expect [Black women] to face [unique barrier] because [mechanism], predicting a negative interaction between race and gender."
- "We do not simply expect additive penalties; we hypothesize that the intersection of [race and class] creates qualitatively distinct [mechanisms]."

**Data requirements**:
- Large samples with sufficient N in all intersection cells (often a problem; rare intersections require oversampling or pooled data)
- When N is small: use descriptive or process-based approaches; avoid overinterpreting interaction coefficients
- Reporting: Always show all main effects AND interaction; report predicted values / margins at each intersection

**Key papers for empirical intersectionality**:
- Browne and Misra (2003 ASR review), Bettie (2003), Crenshaw (1991 SSRN), Grollman (2014 ASR)

### Global / Non-Western Theoretical Traditions

**Why it matters**: Most canonical sociological theory was produced in Western European and North American contexts. Applying these frameworks globally without interrogation can reproduce analytic blind spots.

#### Coloniality and Decolonial Sociology (Quijano; Mignolo; Grosfoguel)

**Coloniality of power** (Quijano 2000):
- Core claim: Colonial racial classification did not end with formal decolonization; it persists in knowledge production, labor organization, gender systems, and state structures
- "Coloniality" (the logic) vs. "colonialism" (the historical period)
- Best for: Global South inequality, race in Latin America, knowledge hierarchies in academia, migrant labor

**Decolonial critique of social science**:
- Western social science uses Europe as the default/universal; "Southern" experiences become deviation or exception
- Call: Use Southern cases as theory-generating, not just theory-testing, sites

**Key works**: Quijano (2000 Latin American Perspectives), Mignolo (2000 Local Histories/Global Designs), Grosfoguel (2011)

---

#### World-Systems Theory (Wallerstein 1974, 1980; Chase-Dunn)

**Core claim**: The capitalist world-economy creates a hierarchical division between core, semi-periphery, and periphery that structures labor, development, and inequality globally.

**Mechanisms**: Unequal exchange; capital extraction from periphery to core; commodity chains; interstate competition

**Best for**: Global economic inequality, development, migration, comparative historical analysis

**Formal hypothesis**: Countries' positions in world-system (core/semi-periphery/periphery) moderate the relationship between [X] and [Y], such that effects found in core nations do not generalize to peripheral nations.

---

#### Postcolonial Theory (Said; Bhabha; Spivak; Fanon)

**Said's Orientalism** (1978): Western knowledge production constructs "the East" as exotic, irrational, and static — a discursive formation that legitimizes domination.
- Best for: Media framing of non-Western populations, immigration discourse, linguistic othering

**Fanon** (1952, 1961): Colonized subjects internalize colonial racial hierarchies; anti-colonial struggle requires psychological and material decolonization.
- Best for: Racial identity, internalized racism, Black consciousness, immigrant identity

**Bhabha's hybridity and mimicry**: Colonial subjects produce hybrid identities; mimicry (adopting the colonizer's culture imperfectly) is a form of resistance.
- Best for: Postcolonial identity, diaspora, language choice

**Spivak's "Can the Subaltern Speak?"** (1988): Marginalized groups are often constituted as incapable of knowledge production; researchers must reflexively examine whose knowledge counts.
- Best for: Research methodology ethics, voice and representation

---

#### Ubuntu and African Relational Philosophy

**"I am because we are"** (Mbiti; Tutu; Metz 2011):
- Ubuntu: Human personhood is constituted through communal relations, not individualism
- Core claim: Well-being, identity, and ethics are fundamentally relational and communal — not an individual property
- Best for: Community-level analyses in Sub-Saharan Africa, collective action, care labor, family support networks
- Contrast with: Western individualism in rational choice models; Putnam's social capital (which treats community as instrumental to individual outcomes)

**Hypothesis language**: "Consistent with Ubuntu philosophy, we hypothesize that [community cohesion / collective obligations] directly shapes [outcomes], independent of individual-level resources."

---

#### Confucian Social Theory and East Asian Sociology

**Key concepts**:
- **Guanxi** (关系): Personal relationship networks that function as social capital with strong obligations; differs from Burt/Granovetter in that guanxi embeds moral obligations and reciprocity beyond information brokerage
- **Face** (Mianzi / Lian): Reputational currency that operates through public recognition and shame; structures status hierarchy and institutional behavior
- **Filial piety** (Xiao): Obligations to parents/ancestors; shapes intergenerational transfers, elder care, fertility decisions
- **Li** (礼): Propriety, ritual, norms; Confucian equivalent of institutional rules

**Best for**: China, Taiwan, Korea, Japan, Chinese diaspora; education decisions, labor market, family obligations, organizational behavior

**Theoretical note**: Guanxi ≠ Western social capital (Lin 2001). Guanxi involves particularistic exchange, not generalized trust; should not be treated as equivalent.

---

#### Southern Theory (Connell 2007; Bhambra 2014)

**Core claim**: Global social science is organized around Northern/Western intellectual centers that extract knowledge from the periphery without reciprocal theoretical integration. "Southern theory" calls for theory-building FROM the global South.

**Implications for research**:
- When applying canonical theories to Southern contexts, treat mismatch as analytically interesting, not as deviation from the universal
- Report when well-established Western findings do NOT replicate in other contexts as a positive contribution
- Cite Southern scholars (not just Western scholars writing about the South)

---

### Integrating Non-Western Frameworks with Canonical Sociology

| Non-Western tradition | Best combined with | Research context |
|----------------------|--------------------|-----------------|
| Coloniality | Stratification, social closure | Race, labor, global inequality |
| World-systems | Organizational sociology | Development, multinational firms |
| Postcolonial | Symbolic boundaries, framing | Immigration discourse, media |
| Ubuntu | Social capital, collective action | Sub-Saharan Africa, diaspora |
| Guanxi/Confucian | Network theory, status attainment | East Asia, Chinese diaspora |
| Intersectionality | Status attainment, segmented assimilation | Race, gender, class in any context |

**Writing tip**: When using non-Western frameworks in a paper targeting Western journals (ASR, AJS), explicitly name the tradition, explain its core claims in 2–3 sentences as you would for any theory, and then derive testable predictions. Reviewers unfamiliar with the tradition need this context.

## Combining Multiple Frameworks

Strong papers often integrate 2 frameworks:
- **Primary framework**: Main causal mechanism
- **Secondary framework**: Moderating condition or boundary specification

Example: "We draw on social capital theory (Lin 2001) to explain the main effect, while incorporating insights from segmented assimilation (Portes and Zhou 1993) to account for heterogeneity across ethnic communities."

**Intersectionality + canonical stratification**:
"We draw on Crenshaw's (1989) intersectionality framework to specify how race and gender jointly shape returns to education, predicting that the wage gap between Black and White workers will be larger for women than men (a negative interaction). This prediction extends status attainment theory (Blau and Duncan 1967) by replacing its additive assumption with an intersectional one."

**Non-Western + canonical**:
"We draw on guanxi theory (Bian 1997) rather than structural holes (Burt 1992) to theorize the mechanism, because guanxi captures the moral obligation embedded in Chinese network ties that Western brokerage models treat as absent."
