# Academic Writing Style Guide for Social Sciences

## Core Principles

### 1. Analytical Precision
Write with analytical precision — say exactly what you mean.

**Vague**: "There is a relationship between education and income."
**Precise**: "Each additional year of schooling is associated with a 9% increase in hourly wages (Card 1999)."

**Vague**: "Social capital helps people."
**Precise**: "Access to high-status professional networks increases the likelihood of obtaining managerial positions by 15 percentage points (Lin 2001)."

### 2. Hedging Appropriately
Match the strength of language to the strength of evidence. **Default to associational language unless the study has a credible causal identification strategy.**

| Evidence Strength | Language | When to use |
|------------------|---------|-------------|
| Experimental / RCT | "X causes Y" / "X increases Y" | Only with randomized assignment |
| Strong quasi-experimental (IV, DiD, RD) | "X leads to Y" / "X generates Y" | Only with explicit identification strategy |
| Standard observational (OLS, logit, FE without causal argument) | "X is associated with Y" / "X predicts Y" / "X is linked to Y" | **DEFAULT for most social science studies** |
| Descriptive / cross-sectional | "X correlates with Y" / "X varies with Y" / "X is patterned by Y" | Descriptive studies, cross-sectional analyses |
| Theoretical claim | "We expect X to be positively related to Y" / "Theory predicts that X and Y should covary" | Hypothesis development |
| Ambiguous finding | "X may be related to Y" / "There is suggestive evidence that..." | Weak or mixed results |

**IMPORTANT**: Do NOT say "proves" for any empirical claim. Do NOT use "effect," "impact" (verb), "influence" (verb), "causes," "leads to," "results in," "drives," or "produces" for non-causal designs. These terms imply a directional causal claim that the research design cannot support. Use "association," "relationship," "pattern," "correlation," or "link" instead of "effect" when describing non-causal findings.

### 3. Active Voice
Use active voice, especially in methods:

**Passive (avoid)**: "Respondents were interviewed..."
**Active (preferred)**: "We interviewed respondents..."

**Passive (avoid)**: "A logistic regression model was estimated..."
**Active (preferred)**: "We estimated a logistic regression model..."

Exception: Describing phenomena where the agent is genuinely unknown:
"Income inequality has risen sharply since the 1970s." (No agent — this is fine.)

### 4. Sentence Structure
- Vary sentence length (mix short punchy with longer analytical)
- Short declarative sentences for key claims: "This paper makes three contributions."
- Longer sentences for mechanism or qualification: "When employers evaluate applicants, they rely not only on formal credentials but also on cultural signals that indicate familiarity with professional norms — a process that systematically disadvantages applicants from working-class backgrounds (Rivera 2012)."
- Avoid sentence fragments
- Avoid run-on sentences joined only by "and"

## Disciplinary Writing Conventions

### Sociology (ASR/AJS)
- **Theoretical grounding is expected**: Every empirical claim connects to theory
- **Citation density**: 2–4 citations per major claim is normal
- **Present tense for claims**: "Granovetter argues that..." (not "argued")
- **Past tense for findings**: "We found that..." / "Our analyses showed..."
- **Jargon is expected but should be defined**: "social reproduction" (Bourdieu 1977), "status attainment" (Blau and Duncan 1967)

**ASR voice characteristics**:
- Confident and assertive
- Theoretical ambition signaled early ("This paper advances our understanding of...")
- Shows awareness of debates in the field
- Does not oversell — "consistent with" rather than "proves"

**AJS voice characteristics**:
- Slightly more literary/essayistic tone acceptable
- Historical depth valued
- Comparative or macro-level arguments common
- More tolerance for theoretical elaboration

### Demography
- **Population focus**: Always anchor claims to a specific population
- **Methodological precision**: Be exact about sample construction, measure operationalization
- **Rates and trends**: Use demographic conventions (per 1,000, TFR, life expectancy at birth)
- **Causal claims cautiously**: Demography journals are conservative about causal language

**Demography voice characteristics**:
- Technical precision
- Explicit about data limitations
- Strong connection to population trends
- Comparative when possible (across countries or time periods)

### Nature Journals
- **Accessible to broad scientific audience**: Avoid sociology-specific jargon without definition
- **Hook for non-specialists**: Opening should convey societal relevance
- **Interdisciplinary framing**: Connect to psychology, economics, public health where relevant
- **Visual-first**: Results told through figures as much as text

**Nature abstract voice**:
Three tight sentences. No wasted words. Each sentence moves from established → new → implication.

## Paragraph Construction

### PEEL Structure
**P**oint (topic sentence): States the main claim of the paragraph
**E**vidence: 1–3 citations or pieces of evidence supporting the claim
**E**xplanation: Unpacks the evidence and explains how it supports the claim
**L**ink: Connects to the next paragraph or the broader argument

**Example**:
"[P] Social networks are not equally accessible to all workers; access varies substantially by race and class background. [E] Black workers are significantly less likely to be connected to high-status professional contacts (Smith 2007; Royster 2003), and working-class individuals rely more heavily on family and neighborhood ties than on the bridging ties that connect workers to distant job opportunities (Lin 2001; Wilson 1996). [E] This unequal distribution of network resources reflects the broader structure of residential segregation and occupational closure that concentrate same-race and same-class ties (Massey and Denton 1993). [L] These structural constraints shape not only who workers know but also what kinds of labor market information and resources are available to them."

## Citation Integration

### Good citation placement
**Wrong**: "Author (2020) found that..."
Use this only when you specifically want to attribute an argument to a specific person.

**Better**: Integrate as support, not as subject:
"Networks provide access to non-redundant information (Granovetter 1973), increasing job search efficiency (Lin 2001)."

**When to use signal phrases** (crediting the author):
- For seminal or contested theoretical arguments: "Bourdieu (1984) famously argues that..."
- When contrasting perspectives: "While Portes (1998) emphasizes..., Putnam (2000) contends that..."
- When discussing empirical findings from a single study: "In a study of 500 US managers, Burt (1992) found that..."

### Citation density norms
- Theory paragraph: 2–5 citations per paragraph (high density expected)
- Methods: 1–2 citations per method decision (cite the estimator)
- Results: Cite only as needed (prior studies for comparison)
- Discussion: 2–4 per paragraph

## Common Writing Errors and Fixes

### Error: "Laundry list" literature review
**Problem**: "Smith (2001) found A. Jones (2003) found B. Chen (2005) found C."
**Fix**: Synthesize: "Collectively, these studies demonstrate that [pattern] across [contexts] (Smith 2001; Jones 2003; Chen 2005), though findings diverge when [condition]."

### Error: Throat-clearing introduction
**Problem**: "In this paper, I will discuss the relationship between X and Y, which is an important topic in sociology because..."
**Fix**: Start with the substantive claim or hook, not with meta-commentary about the paper.

### Error: Overusing "important" and "significant"
**Problem**: "This is an important finding with significant implications..."
**Fix**: Specify *why* and *for whom*: "This finding suggests that programs targeting [population] should prioritize [mechanism], given that [implication]."

### Error: Conflating theoretical and empirical claims
**Problem**: "Social capital causes upward mobility (Granovetter 1973)."
**Fix**: Separate theory from evidence: "Granovetter (1973) theorizes that weak ties increase job search efficiency. Consistent with this argument, studies find that workers with access to diverse contacts obtain higher-wage jobs (Lin 2001; McDonald 2011)."

### Error: Using scare quotes
**Problem**: "...what some call 'class warfare'..."
**Fix**: Either use the term straightforwardly or define it: "'Class conflict,' as Weber (1978) defines it, refers to struggles over the distribution of economic resources."

### Error: Burying the lede
**Problem**: 3 paragraphs of context before stating the research question
**Fix**: State the puzzle and research question in paragraph 1 or 2.

## Word Choice Reference

| Avoid | Prefer |
|-------|--------|
| "shows" | "demonstrates," "reveals," "indicates" |
| "looks at" | "examines," "investigates," "analyzes" |
| "talks about" | "argues," "contends," "theorizes" |
| "a lot of" | "substantial," "considerable," "many" |
| "prove" | "demonstrate," "provide evidence that" |
| "very important" | "central," "crucial," "pivotal" |
| "in today's society" | specify context: "in contemporary United States" |
| "overall" (to start) | begin with substantive claim |
| "In conclusion, this paper has..." | Begin conclusion with synthesis, not meta-statement |

## Section-Specific Verb Tense Guide

| Section | Tense | Example |
|---------|-------|---------|
| Introduction (general claims) | Present | "Inequality remains persistent in..." |
| Literature review (existing findings) | Present | "Granovetter (1973) argues..." |
| Methods (what you did) | Past | "We collected data from..." |
| Results (what you found) | Past | "We found that X was associated with Y..." |
| Discussion (interpreting) | Present | "These findings suggest that..." |
| Describing tables/figures | Present | "Table 1 presents descriptive statistics..." |

## Nature-Style Writing Guide

### Introduction (no "Literature Review" heading)

- All background is integrated into the Introduction — there is no separate Background or Theory section
- Open with societal relevance before disciplinary debate
- End with: "Here we [show / find / demonstrate] that [main finding], using [data/method]."
- Keep theoretical machinery lean: one core claim, not a survey of competing perspectives
- Length: 500–700 words (NHB/NCS), 800–1,200 words (Science Advances)

### Results Subsection Headings

Use active, declarative headings that state the finding — not model numbers:

| Avoid | Prefer |
|-------|--------|
| "Analysis 1" | "Redlining predicts lower activity-space diversity" |
| "Main results" | "The segregation penalty is largest in Southern cities" |
| "Model 3" | "Effects persist after controlling for contemporary income" |

Each Results subsection should:
1. State the key finding in the first sentence
2. Report statistics with effect size + uncertainty (95% CI preferred over SE alone)
3. Reference figure or table
4. Close with one interpretive sentence connecting back to the argument

### Methods Section (after Discussion)

- Can be more technical than sociology methods sections
- Subsections: **Data** → **Measures** → **Statistical Analyses** → **Reporting Summary**
- Use past tense throughout
- State pre-registration or power analysis details here
- Note data and code availability in this section

### Reporting Standards (NHB/NCS)

- Report exact p-values, not ranges: `p = 0.023`, not `p < .05`
- Report effect sizes with 95% CIs for all key estimates
- Include sample size in each key result statement
- Mention the most important robustness check in the main text (not only in the appendix)
- Reference list: maximum 50 items

## Revision Mode: Common Reviewer Concerns

### "The contribution is unclear"

**Fix**:
- Rewrite the final paragraph of the Introduction with an explicit three-part statement:
  > "This paper contributes to [literature] by (1) providing the first [nationally representative / longitudinal / causal] evidence that [specific finding], (2) identifying [mechanism] as the operative pathway, and (3) demonstrating that [scope condition / boundary]."
- Replace vague framing ("This paper examines...") with specific advance framing ("We advance this literature by...").

---

### "The mechanism is underspecified"

**Fix**:
- Add a dedicated mechanism paragraph in the Theory section (use the Mechanism Elaboration template in `paper-structure.md`)
- Name the mechanism with a specific label: "I call this the [X] mechanism"
- Spell out the causal chain: X → M₁ → M₂ → Y
- Cite empirical support for each step separately

---

### "The literature review is too broad / not well synthesized"

**Fix**:
- Replace summary sentences ("Smith (2001) found A. Jones (2003) found B.") with synthesis sentences:
  > "Collectively, these studies demonstrate that [pattern] across [contexts] (Smith 2001; Jones 2003), though findings diverge when [condition]."
- Apply the PEEL structure (see Paragraph Construction section) to every background paragraph
- Cut studies that do not directly inform the theoretical argument

---

### "The causal claim is too strong for the data"

**Fix**:
- Audit every instance of "causes," "leads to," "increases" and replace with "is associated with," "predicts," "is linked to"
- Add one explicit hedging sentence in the Discussion:
  > "While we cannot rule out [specific confound], [reason why the estimate is nonetheless informative — e.g., 'the sensitivity analysis shows our estimate would require a confounder explaining X% of residual variance to be explained away']."
- Run and report a directed robustness check targeting the most plausible confound

---

### "The discussion merely restates results"

**Fix**:
- Add one "theoretical interpretation" paragraph per main finding starting with:
  > "What does this finding mean for [theory]? [Explanation in theoretical terms]."
- Add one explicit comparison-to-prior-work sentence per major finding:
  > "Our results corroborate / extend / challenge [Author Year], who found [their finding]. [Explain how yours deepens or qualifies theirs]."
- Strengthen the scope conditions paragraph: for whom and where does this finding hold, and why?

## Transition Sentence Library

### Within-Section Transitions (Theory)

- **Moving to the next mechanism**: "Beyond [first mechanism], [second mechanism] also shapes [outcome]."
- **Introducing moderation**: "Importantly, this effect is not uniform — it varies by [moderator]."
- **Ruling out a rival**: "A rival explanation might propose [alternative]. However, [reason this is less likely given the design / data]."
- **Deriving a hypothesis**: "This theoretical reasoning generates our first hypothesis:"
- **Moving to scope conditions**: "This argument is most applicable in [context], where [condition] holds."

### Between-Section Transitions

- **Introduction → Theory**: "Below, we develop a theoretical framework to explain [phenomenon]. We then describe our data and analytic approach before presenting results."
- **Theory → Methods**: "To test these hypotheses, we draw on [data source], which [key advantage for this test]."
- **Methods → Results**: "We turn now to the empirical results."
- **Results → Discussion**: "In the Discussion, we interpret these findings in light of [theoretical framework] and consider their broader implications."

### Paragraph-to-Paragraph Transitions (Background/Theory)

- **Adding supporting evidence**: "Consistent with this argument, [Author Year] find that [evidence]."
- **Introducing tension**: "Yet this pattern does not hold uniformly. [Author Year] show that among [subgroup], [divergent finding]."
- **Narrowing to the gap**: "Despite this body of work, no study has examined [specific gap]. This gap matters because [reason]."
- **Flagging the paper's extension**: "Our paper extends this line of work by [how]."

### Discussion Transitions

- **Opening**: "This paper examined [topic] using [data/design]. We find [summary of key finding]."
- **Moving to interpretation**: "What explains this pattern? We argue that [mechanism]."
- **Moving to limitations**: "These findings should be interpreted in light of several limitations."
- **Closing**: "In sum, [main conclusion], with implications for [theory and/or policy]."
