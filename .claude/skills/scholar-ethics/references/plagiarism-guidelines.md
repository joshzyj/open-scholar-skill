# Plagiarism and Originality Guidelines

Reference guide for `scholar-ethics` MODE 2.

---

## 1. Types of Plagiarism

### 1.1 Verbatim Plagiarism
Copying text word-for-word without quotation marks or attribution.

- Even three or more consecutive words from a source without attribution is a risk
- Most common in literature review sections where summaries of prior work are paraphrased too closely
- Detection: iThenticate, Turnitin, CrossRef Similarity Check

### 1.2 Patchwork (Mosaic) Plagiarism
Paraphrasing closely with minor word substitutions (synonym swapping) without attribution.

**Original:** "Residential segregation concentrates poverty in urban neighborhoods and limits residents' access to quality schools."
**Patchwork:** "Neighborhood segregation clusters poverty in cities and restricts residents' access to good schools."

This still constitutes plagiarism even though no sentence is verbatim.

### 1.3 Self-Plagiarism / Text Recycling
Reusing substantial portions of your own prior work without disclosure.

**Permitted:**
- Citing your own prior work as a source
- Brief overlap in methods descriptions with explicit citation ("Following [Author Year]...")
- Conference paper → journal article with disclosed overlap

**Not permitted:**
- Submitting the same paper to two journals simultaneously (duplicate submission)
- Reproducing large sections from a prior article without attribution
- Reproducing your own dissertation chapter as an article without noting the connection

### 1.4 Idea Plagiarism
Presenting someone else's original research question, theoretical framework, or conceptual model as your own innovation without attribution.

### 1.5 Data Plagiarism
Using another researcher's dataset (non-public) without permission or attribution, or presenting their data collection as your own.

### 1.6 AI-Generated Plagiarism
Submitting AI-generated text that may silently reproduce training data sources without citation. AI models can generate passages very similar to existing published text without flagging the source.

**Risk:**
- AI-generated literature reviews may reproduce exact phrases from papers without attribution
- AI-generated citations may hallucinate real-sounding but nonexistent references
- Solution: verify every citation generated by AI before submission; do not submit unverified AI text

## 2. Journal Plagiarism Policies

| Journal | Tool used | Threshold / Policy |
|---------|-----------|------------------|
| **Nature / NHB / NCS** | iThenticate (CrossRef) | Similarity report required at submission; editors investigate any significant overlap |
| **Science** | iThenticate | Similarity report screened by editors |
| **Science Advances** | iThenticate | Overlap screened; prior preprint OK with note |
| **PNAS** | Automated check | Editors flag high-similarity submissions |
| **ASR** | Varies by submission | ASA Code of Ethics applies; dual submission prohibited |
| **AJS** | Editorial check | Standard norms; no duplicate publication |
| **Demography** | Editorial check | Preprint OK; disclosed conference paper OK |
| **PLOS ONE** | iThenticate | Automated report reviewed by editors |
| **Social Forces** | CrossRef Similarity | Significant overlap investigated |

**CrossRef Similarity Check** covers ~100 million published articles + preprints. Most COPE member journals use it.

## 3. COPE Guidelines on Text Recycling

The **Committee on Publication Ethics (COPE)** distinguishes:

**Acceptable text recycling:**
- Methods sections: standard descriptions of widely-used methods (e.g., "We estimated logistic regression models using maximum likelihood") — no citation needed
- Short transitional phrases across papers by same authors — disclose
- Data description from a prior paper that uses the same dataset — must cite prior paper

**Unacceptable text recycling:**
- Extended passages (> 1 paragraph) reproduced without quotation or citation, even from your own prior work
- Identical results sections across two papers (dual publication)
- Reproducing prior paper's introduction without noting it is a follow-up study

**COPE guidance** (publicationethics.org/text-recycling): Authors should disclose all prior overlapping publications at submission. Journals should not accept papers with undisclosed overlap.

## 4. Self-Plagiarism: Common Scenarios in Social Science

### Scenario 1: Conference paper → journal article
**Status:** Acceptable with disclosure
**Action:** Cite the conference paper in the manuscript; note in cover letter ("An earlier version of this paper was presented at ASA 2024.")

### Scenario 2: Dissertation chapter → journal article
**Status:** Acceptable with disclosure
**Action:** Cite the dissertation; note in cover letter and Acknowledgments ("This paper draws on the author's doctoral dissertation [title, institution, year].")

### Scenario 3: Working paper → journal article
**Status:** Acceptable; preprints encouraged
**Action:** Note preprint version in cover letter; update preprint if revisions are substantial

### Scenario 4: Journal article A and journal article B use the same dataset
**Status:** Acceptable if papers address different RQs and are disclosed to editors
**Action:** Cite the companion paper; cover letter should note "Companion paper [Author Year] addresses [related but different] research question using the same dataset."

### Scenario 5: Two journal articles with overlapping theory sections
**Status:** Unacceptable without attribution
**Action:** Either cite the prior paper and rephrase, or rewrite the theory section independently

### Scenario 6: Methods section is nearly identical to a prior paper
**Status:** Borderline; common in sociology with panel datasets
**Action:** Cite the prior paper ("This study uses data and cleaning procedures identical to [Author Year]. See that paper for full details.")

## 5. AI-Generated Text Policies by Journal (as of 2025)

| Journal | Policy |
|---------|--------|
| **Nature** | Prohibited as "author"; use must be disclosed in Methods; AI cannot meet authorship criteria |
| **NHB / NCS** | Same as Nature; required disclosure in submission checklist |
| **Science** | Must disclose in Methods if AI used for writing/editing |
| **Science Advances** | Disclose AI writing tools in Methods or Acknowledgments |
| **PNAS** | Disclose AI assistance in Acknowledgments |
| **ASR / AJS** | No formal policy as of 2025; ASA Code of Ethics requires honest representation; disclose in Methods note |
| **Demography** | No formal policy; disclose as emerging norm |
| **PLOS ONE** | AI use must be declared in submission |
| **Cell Press** | AI tools prohibited from writing, edits, or image creation without disclosure |

**Universal rule (ICMJE, COPE, CSE):**
- AI tools cannot be listed as authors
- AI-assisted text must be disclosed
- Authors remain responsible for accuracy and integrity of all content, including AI-assisted content

## 6. Similarity Score Interpretation Guide

### iThenticate / Turnitin score breakdown

| Score | Action |
|-------|---------  |
| 0–10% | Typically acceptable; expected overlap with cited sources, common phrases, references |
| 10–20% | Review flagged passages; likely fine if all text is attributed; watch for unlicensed method descriptions |
| 20–30% | Investigate all highlighted passages; check for self-recycling of prior work; may require revision |
| >30% | Serious concern; detailed review required; re-write flagged sections or clearly attribute |

### What to exclude from similarity calculation
Always instruct iThenticate/Turnitin to exclude:
- ✓ References / bibliography
- ✓ Quoted text (attributed block quotes)
- ✓ Small matches (< 10 words)
- ✓ Your own submitted prior work (compare to self only, not flag)

### False positives
These are commonly flagged but are not plagiarism:
- "We used a fixed-effects model to account for unobserved heterogeneity" — standard methods language
- "The data come from the [Survey Name], a nationally representative sample of..."
- APA / ASA in-text citation format: "(Author, Year)" — appears similar to other papers
- Variable names and statistical notation

## 7. Proper Attribution Quick Reference

| Situation | How to attribute |
|-----------|----------------|
| Direct quote (< 40 words) | "Quote" (Author Year:page) or (Author Year, p. X) |
| Direct quote (> 40 words) | Block indent; (Author Year:page) at end |
| Paraphrase | (Author Year) at end of paraphrase sentence |
| Idea from a source | "According to Author (Year)..." or (Author Year) |
| Statistical finding from a source | "Author (Year) found that N% of respondents..." + (Author Year) |
| Your own prior work | "[In our earlier work / Following Author Year]..." |
| Survey instrument adapted from another | "Adapted from [Survey Name] (Author Year)" in footnote or methods |
| Figure reproduced from another paper | "Reprinted/Adapted from [Title], by [Author], [Year], [Journal], [volume](issue), p. X. Copyright [year] by [publisher]. Reprinted with permission." |

## 8. Originality Statement Templates

**Standard originality statement (cover letter):**
> I confirm that this manuscript is original work not previously published, in whole or in part, and is not currently under consideration at any other journal. Prior versions presented at conferences are cited in the text. Any AI writing assistance used is disclosed in the manuscript.

**For companion paper using same dataset:**
> This manuscript analyzes data also used in our companion paper [Author et al., Year, Journal]. The current paper addresses a distinct research question ([RQ]). Overlap in data description is minimized and attributed to the companion paper.

**For paper based on dissertation:**
> This paper is based on a chapter from the first author's doctoral dissertation ([Title], [Institution], [Year]). The dissertation is cited in the text.
