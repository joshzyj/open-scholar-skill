# Interview Protocol Templates and Fieldwork Reference

## Full Semi-Structured Interview Protocol Template

```
PROJECT: [Study title]
INTERVIEWER: _______________
DATE / TIME: _______________
PARTICIPANT ID: _______________
LOCATION: _______________
DURATION: _______________

─────────────────────────────────────
PREAMBLE (Read aloud)
─────────────────────────────────────
"Thank you for agreeing to speak with me today. I'm [name], a researcher
at [institution]. The purpose of this study is to [brief, accessible
description of study purpose]. There are no right or wrong answers — I'm
interested in your own experiences and perspectives.

This interview will take approximately [time]. I'd like to record our
conversation so I can accurately capture what you say. The recording will
be transcribed and then deleted. Your name will not appear in any
publication — I will use a pseudonym. You can stop the interview at any
time, and you can skip any question you don't want to answer.

[If relevant]: Some of the topics we'll discuss today may bring up
difficult memories. If at any point you want to take a break, just let
me know.

Do you have any questions before we begin? [Pause] Do I have your
permission to record? [Start recorder]

─────────────────────────────────────
SECTION 1: BACKGROUND / RAPPORT OPENER
─────────────────────────────────────
1. Can you start by telling me a bit about yourself — where you grew up,
   how you ended up [in this city / in this job / in this situation]?

   Probes:
   - How long have you been here?
   - What brought you here?

─────────────────────────────────────
SECTION 2: [MAIN THEME 1]
─────────────────────────────────────
2. Grand tour: "Can you walk me through what a typical [day / week /
   encounter with X] looks like for you?"

   Probes:
   - Tell me more about [specific element they mentioned]
   - What happens next?
   - Who else is involved in that?

3. Critical incident: "Can you think of a specific time when [phenomenon]
   happened? Tell me what happened."

   Probes:
   - What were you thinking at that moment?
   - How did you feel?
   - What did you do?
   - How did it turn out?

─────────────────────────────────────
SECTION 3: [MAIN THEME 2]
─────────────────────────────────────
4. [Theme 2 question — follow same pattern]

─────────────────────────────────────
SECTION 4: CONTRAST AND COMPARISON
─────────────────────────────────────
5. "How is your experience with [X] similar to or different from [other
   people you know / before / in another context]?"

6. "Some people I've talked with describe [X] in terms of [common theme].
   Does that resonate with your experience, or would you describe it
   differently?"

─────────────────────────────────────
SECTION 5: MEANING AND INTERPRETATION
─────────────────────────────────────
7. "Looking back, what do you think accounts for [outcome / pattern you
   have described]?"

8. "If you could change one thing about [situation], what would it be
   and why?"

─────────────────────────────────────
CLOSING
─────────────────────────────────────
9. "Is there anything about your experience with [topic] that I haven't
   asked about that you think is important for me to understand?"

10. "Is there anyone else you think I should talk with — someone whose
    perspective might be different from yours?"

[If sensitive topics were covered]:
"We covered some difficult topics today. How are you feeling? I want to
make sure you have [resource card / number for support services] in case
anything comes up after our conversation."

"Thank you so much for your time. Is there anything you'd like to ask me
about the study?"
[Stop recorder]
```

---

## Protocol Design Checklist

- [ ] Opening is rapport-building, low-stakes, biographical
- [ ] Grand tour question comes before specific probes
- [ ] Questions are open-ended (begin with "How," "Can you tell me," "What")
- [ ] No leading questions ("Don't you think that...")
- [ ] Each section has 2–3 core questions + specific probes for each
- [ ] Critical incident question included for key phenomena
- [ ] Contrast/comparison question to elicit variation
- [ ] Closing asks for what was missed + referrals for more participants
- [ ] Debriefing / resource-sharing plan for sensitive topics
- [ ] Estimated total duration tested in pilot interview

## Sampling Strategy Reference

### Purposive Sampling
Select cases to maximize relevant variation on dimensions that matter theoretically.

**Maximum variation sampling**: Include cases that represent the full range of variation
- e.g., for study of immigrant labor market integration: sample from multiple national-origin groups, education levels, time-since-arrival strata, industry sectors

**Homogeneous sampling**: Select cases that are similar on key characteristics to study a phenomenon in depth
- e.g., all working-class Puerto Rican women in New York in the same occupation

**Critical case sampling**: Select cases that are crucial tests of the theoretical argument
- e.g., select the one case that, if it doesn't fit the theory, challenges it most severely

**Deviant/extreme case sampling**: Select unusual cases that might reveal mechanisms not visible in typical cases

### Theoretical Sampling (Grounded Theory)
- Begin with a small sample; analyze early interviews; identify emerging concepts
- Sample next interviews to develop, refine, or challenge those emerging concepts
- Continue until **theoretical saturation**: new interviews add no new conceptual categories

Saturation typically occurs at N = 15–30 for in-depth interviews in a single population; may require more for comparative work across groups.

## Transcription Standards

**Verbatim transcription** (standard for sociolinguistics; recommended for sociology):
- Include all words, filled pauses ("um," "uh"), false starts, laughter
- Mark overlaps, interruptions, pauses
- Note nonverbal behavior in brackets: [laughs], [long pause], [becomes tearful]

**Clean verbatim transcription** (adequate for most sociological interview analysis):
- All words included
- False starts and fillers omitted for readability
- Pauses noted only if they seem meaningful
- Still mark [laughs], [interruption], [inaudible]

**Anonymization during transcription**:
- Replace all names with [FNAME], [LNAME] or pseudonyms in transcription
- Replace employer names, specific street addresses, school names
- Note at top of transcript: all names are pseudonyms; locations generalized

## Field Notes Template

Written within 24 hours of observation or interview:

```
FIELD NOTE
Date: [Date and time]
Location: [Place — anonymized]
Duration: [hours:minutes]
Observer/Interviewer: [your ID]

DESCRIPTIVE NOTES (what happened — as concrete and specific as possible):
[Who was present? What did people do and say? Physical setting?
Sequence of events? Direct observations — not interpretations]

METHODOLOGICAL NOTES (how the data collection went):
[How was rapport? What went well? What should I do differently?
Was the protocol adequate? Any unexpected access issues?]

ANALYTIC NOTES (preliminary interpretations and hunches):
[What patterns am I noticing? What theoretical concepts does this
connect to? What questions does this raise?]

PERSONAL NOTES (researcher's feelings, reactions, biases):
[How did I feel during this encounter? Did my identity/position
affect what I observed? What surprised me?]
```
