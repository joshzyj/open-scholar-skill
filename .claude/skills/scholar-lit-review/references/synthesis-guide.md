# Literature Synthesis Frameworks

## Synthesis vs. Summary

**Summary**: "Paper A found X. Paper B found Y. Paper C found Z."
**Synthesis**: "Collectively, papers A, B, and C suggest that when [condition], X obtains, but when [alternative condition], Y or Z is more likely."

Synthesis identifies patterns, tensions, and relationships across sources. Always aim for synthesis.

## Argumentative Structures for Lit Reviews

### Structure 1: Theoretical Debate
Best for: Topics with competing theoretical explanations

```
Opening: "Two broad explanations have been proposed for [phenomenon]..."
Section 1: Theory A and its evidence (3–5 papers)
Section 2: Theory B and its evidence (3–5 papers)
Tension: "These perspectives make conflicting predictions about [X]..."
Gap: "Despite this debate, no study has directly compared the two explanations using [design/population]."
```

### Structure 2: Historical Development
Best for: Mature fields with clear intellectual lineage

```
Early work: "Early research on [topic] established..."
Shift: "By the 1980s, scholars began to question..."
Current consensus: "The dominant view now holds that..."
Frontier: "Recent work has begun to examine..."
Gap: "However, [specific limitation] remains unresolved."
```

### Structure 3: Empirical Landscape
Best for: Topics where findings vary by context/population

```
What we know broadly: "Across settings, [core finding] is well-established..."
Variation: "However, effects vary substantially by [group A vs. B] / [context 1 vs. 2]..."
Mechanisms: "This variation is attributed to [mechanism 1, 2, 3]..."
Gap: "Missing from this literature is attention to [population/context/mechanism]."
```

### Structure 4: Methodological Critique
Best for: Papers with a methodological contribution

```
Prior findings: "Past studies consistently find..."
Methodological limitations: "However, these studies rely on [limitation: cross-sectional data, self-report, selection bias]..."
What we still don't know: "Without [better design], it is unclear whether [findings reflect X or confound Y]..."
Your contribution: "This study addresses this limitation by [using exogenous variation / longitudinal data / etc.]."
```

### Structure 5: Interdisciplinary Bridge
Best for: Topics spanning sociology and an adjacent field (economics, political science, public health, psychology)

```
Opening: "While [field A] has focused on [dimension X], [field B] has emphasized [dimension Y]..."
Field A contribution: "[Discipline A] has established that [finding], using [typical designs]..."
Field B contribution: "[Discipline B] adds that [finding], using [different designs]..."
Integration point: "These literatures converge on [shared finding] but diverge on [contested issue]..."
Gap: "Neither tradition has examined [the intersection / the mechanism / the population] that we study."
Your bridge: "By combining [insight from A] with [method/data from B], this paper [contribution]..."
```

### Structure 6: Computational/Methodological Innovation
Best for: Papers introducing new methods (NLP, ML, network analysis) to established questions

```
Substantive question: "[Important social science question] has been studied for decades..."
Traditional approach: "The dominant approach relies on [surveys, experiments, administrative data]..."
Limitation of traditional approach: "This approach is limited by [scale, measurement, ecological validity, temporal resolution]..."
Computational opportunity: "Recent advances in [NLP/ML/network analysis/computational methods] offer [new capability]..."
Prior computational work: "A growing literature has applied [method] to [adjacent question], showing [results]..."
Gap: "No study has yet used [method] to examine [your specific question], despite its promise for [specific advantage]."
Your contribution: "We apply [specific technique] to [data source], allowing us to [specific advance]."
```

## Landscape Map Narrative Templates

Use these templates to write up each dimension of the Literature Landscape Map (Phase 4) as flowing prose.

### Field Evolution Narrative Template

Use when writing up dimension 4a (Field Evolution Timeline):

```
The study of [topic] has undergone significant transformation since [foundational Author (Year)]
first [established/proposed/demonstrated] [key insight]. In the [decade], the field was dominated
by [paradigm/approach], with scholars focusing on [what they studied] using [dominant methods]
(Author Year; Author Year; Author Year).

A major shift occurred in the [decade] when [Author (Year)] [challenged/extended/reframed]
this view by [what they did differently]. This work [opened new questions / resolved a puzzle /
introduced a new variable], prompting a wave of studies examining [new focus area]
(Author Year; Author Year).

The contemporary landscape is characterized by [consensus on X / ongoing debate about Y /
fragmentation into Z subfields]. Recent work has [trend 1] (Author Year), [trend 2]
(Author Year), and [trend 3] (Author Year). Despite these advances, [what remains unresolved].
```

### Debate Mapping Template

Use when the theoretical landscape (4b) features competing theories:

```
DEBATE MAP: [Theory A] vs. [Theory B]

Position A: [one-sentence summary]
  Core prediction: [what A predicts about X→Y]
  Key evidence: [Author Year found...; Author Year confirmed with...]
  Scope conditions: [where/when A holds best]
  Weakness: [where A fails or cannot explain]

Position B: [one-sentence summary]
  Core prediction: [what B predicts about X→Y]
  Key evidence: [Author Year showed...; Author Year demonstrated...]
  Scope conditions: [where/when B holds best]
  Weakness: [where B fails or cannot explain]

Points of convergence: [what both traditions agree on]
Points of divergence: [where they make opposite predictions]
Unresolved: [what would settle the debate — design, data, population]
```

### Mechanism Narrative Template

Use when writing up dimension 4f (Mechanisms Inventory):

```
The literature has proposed [N] primary mechanisms through which [X] may affect [Y].

First, [Mechanism 1 — name and type]. [Author (Year)] argues that [explanation of the mechanism].
[Supporting evidence: Author (Year) tested this directly using... and found...]. However,
[limitation or qualification: Author (Year) shows this holds only when...].

Second, [Mechanism 2]. [Author (Year)] proposes that [explanation]. Unlike [Mechanism 1],
this pathway operates through [different channel]. Empirical support is [strong/mixed/limited]:
[Author (Year)] found [result], but [Author (Year)] found [contradictory result when...].

Of these mechanisms, [which have been directly tested vs. merely assumed]. The distinction
matters because [why it matters for this research question].
```

### Gap Narrative Template

Use when writing up dimension 4h (Research Gaps Summary):

```
This review identifies [N] key gaps in the existing literature, ranked by their potential
to advance theoretical understanding and empirical knowledge.

The most consequential gap concerns [Gap 1 — specific statement]. The closest prior work
is [Author (Year)], who [what they did]. However, [Author (Year)] [specific limitation:
did not examine [population], used [limited design], could not test [mechanism]]. This gap
matters because [theoretical or policy stakes].

A second gap involves [Gap 2]. While [what is known], no study has [what is missing].
[Author (Year)] comes closest but [limitation]. Addressing this gap would require
[data/method/design needed].
```

## Transition Phrases for Synthesis

**To introduce consensus**:
- "A well-established finding in this literature is..."
- "Scholars broadly agree that..."
- "Decades of research consistently show..."
- "Across diverse samples and designs, the evidence converges on..."

**To introduce disagreement**:
- "Yet this consensus has been challenged by..."
- "However, a body of work complicates this view..."
- "The evidence is less clear when it comes to..."
- "Despite this, [X] remains contested because..."
- "Not all studies confirm this pattern..."

**To introduce your contribution**:
- "What remains underexamined is..."
- "A critical gap in this literature concerns..."
- "No study has yet addressed..."
- "This paper addresses this gap by..."
- "We advance this literature by..."

**To connect mechanisms**:
- "The mechanism underlying this relationship is..."
- "This effect is thought to operate through..."
- "Several processes may explain why..."
- "The pathway from [X] to [Y] runs through..."

**To mark temporal evolution**:
- "Early work on [topic] focused on..."
- "A turning point came when..."
- "More recently, scholars have shifted attention to..."
- "The latest generation of studies examines..."

**To bridge disciplines**:
- "While sociologists have emphasized [X], economists have focused on [Y]..."
- "Drawing on insights from [adjacent field]..."
- "This interdisciplinary perspective suggests..."
- "Integrating [field A]'s emphasis on [X] with [field B]'s attention to [Y]..."

**To acknowledge null/negative findings**:
- "Notably, [Author (Year)] found no significant relationship..."
- "This null finding is informative because..."
- "The absence of an effect in [context] suggests that..."

## Citation Conventions

### Grouping citations
When multiple papers support the same claim, list them chronologically:
> "Prior research consistently finds that education predicts earnings (Mincer 1974; Jencks et al. 1979; Card 1999; Goldin and Katz 2008)."

### Crediting specific arguments
When attributing a specific theoretical argument to an author:
> "Granovetter (1973) argues that weak ties serve as bridges..."
> "According to Bourdieu (1984), cultural capital is transmitted through..."

### Contested claims
When evidence is mixed, acknowledge both sides:
> "Some studies find positive effects (Author1 Year; Author2 Year), while others report null or negative results (Author3 Year; Author4 Year)."

### Qualitative vs. quantitative findings
Note when findings come from different methodological traditions:
> "Ethnographic studies highlight [pattern] (Duneier 1999; Goffman 2014), a finding corroborated by survey data (Author Year)."

### Citing review articles
When using an Annual Review or meta-analysis as a source:
> "As [Author (Year)] documents in a comprehensive review of [N] studies..."
> "A recent meta-analysis of [N] studies finds an average effect of [d = X] (Author Year)."

## Gap Identification Checklist

After reviewing the literature, ask:

**Population gaps**:
- Has this phenomenon been studied among [underrepresented group: immigrants, racial minorities, women, elderly, low-SES]?
- Do findings from the US generalize to other countries?
- Has the relationship changed over cohorts or historical periods?
- Are intersectional populations (e.g., low-income immigrants, elderly women of color) examined?

**Mechanism gaps**:
- What explains why X causes Y? Has the mechanism been directly tested?
- Are there moderating conditions (who, when, where) not yet examined?
- Is there reverse causation that has not been addressed?
- Have competing mechanisms been distinguished empirically?

**Methodological gaps**:
- Do existing studies rely on cross-sectional data when longitudinal is needed?
- Is there selection bias that has not been addressed causally?
- Have key variables been measured with error?
- Has anyone used [new data source, new method, computational approach]?
- Are sample sizes adequate for detecting small but meaningful effects?

**Theoretical gaps**:
- Do competing theories make untested predictions?
- Have theories been tested in all relevant scope conditions?
- Are there theoretical contradictions that remain unresolved?
- Has anyone integrated insights from multiple theoretical traditions?

**Temporal gaps**:
- When were the foundational studies conducted? Has the world changed since?
- Do findings from the 1990s still hold in the 2020s?
- Are there period effects, cohort effects, or institutional changes that might alter the relationship?

## Example Synthesis Paragraph (Stratification)

> "A large body of research has demonstrated that parental socioeconomic status shapes children's educational attainment through multiple pathways (Blau and Duncan 1967; Sewell, Haller, and Portes 1969; Jencks et al. 1979). Beyond the material resources that affluent families provide, scholars have increasingly emphasized cultural mechanisms, including familiarity with elite institutional norms (Lareau 2003), access to educational expectations and information (Morgan 2005), and the differential signaling value of educational credentials across social contexts (Hout 2012). Despite this progress, less attention has been paid to how digital information environments may alter these transmission pathways. The explosion of online information about colleges and scholarships could plausibly reduce informational advantages among high-SES families—or, alternatively, may benefit those with greater digital capital (Reardon 2011). This paper examines whether internet access moderates the socioeconomic gradient in college enrollment decisions."

## Example Synthesis Paragraph (Computational Innovation)

> "Traditional studies of political polarization have relied on survey measures of ideological self-placement and voting behavior (Fiorina, Abrams, and Pope 2005; Abramowitz and Saunders 2008). While these studies establish that partisan sorting has increased, they cannot capture the fine-grained dynamics of how polarization unfolds in everyday discourse. Recent computational approaches have begun to address this limitation: Bail et al. (2018) used a field experiment on Twitter to show that exposure to opposing views can increase polarization, while Barberá (2015) estimated ideological positions from social media following networks. These studies demonstrate the power of digital trace data for studying political attitudes at scale. However, no study has applied large language models to analyze the *content* of cross-partisan interactions, a gap that limits our understanding of which discursive strategies exacerbate versus mitigate polarization."
